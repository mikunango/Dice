CH18- Tribunals

Part XIV-A — Article 323A - 323B

Tribunals: A tribunal is a quasi-judicial institution that deals with the resolution of disputes pertaining to administration, taxation, environment, securities, etc.

The original Constitution did not contain provisions with respect to tribunals. The 42nd Amendment Act of 1976 added a new Part XIV-A to the Constitution.

ARTICLE 323A

Empowers the Parliament to provide for the establishment of administrative tribunals for the adjudication of disputes relating to recruitment and conditions of service of persons appointed to public services of the Centre, the states, local bodies, public corporations, and other public authorities.

  • 1. Parliament Enacted: ✼ The Parliament enacted the Administrative Tribunals Act in 1985, which empowers the Central government to establish the Central Administrative Tribunal and state-level administrative tribunals.
ARTICLE 323B

Empowers the Parliament and state legislatures to set up tribunals for various matters like Industrial and labour, Foreign exchange, import and export, Land reforms, Food, The ceiling on urban property, Elections to Parliament and state legislatures, Rent and tenancy rights, etc.

FEATURES
  • 1. Natural Justice: Tribunals the principle of natural justice, which requires that all parties to a legal proceeding be given a fair and impartial hearing and that no person should be a judge in their own cause.
  • 2. Not Bound by Strict Rules: Tribunals are not bound by the strict rules of procedure and evidence set out in the CPC.
  • 3. Specialized Expertise: It is comprised of members who have specialized expertise in the subject matter over which they have jurisdiction.
  • 4. Binding Decisions: Tribunals can hear evidence, examine witnesses, make findings of fact, apply the law to the facts, and make binding decisions.
  • 5. Appeal: The decisions of tribunals can be appealed to a higher court.
  • 6. Time-bound: Time-bound resolution.
SIGNIFICANCE
  • 1. Reduce Workload: The Tribunals were set up to reduce the workload of courts, to expedite decisions and to provide a forum which would be manned by lawyers and experts in the areas falling under the jurisdiction of the Tribunal.

ADMINISTRATIVE TRIBUNALS
CENTRAL ADMINISTRATIVE TRIBUNAL (CAT)
  • 1. Jurisdiction: Related to recruitment and all service matters of public servants covered by it.
  • 2. Extent: Its jurisdiction extends to the All-India services, the Central civil services, civil posts under the Centre, and civilian employees of defense services.
  • 3. Exclusions: However, the members of the defense forces, officers and servants of the Supreme Court, and the secretarial staff of the Parliament are not covered by it.
  • 4. Appointment: The chairman and the members of CAT are appointed by the President after consultation with the Chief Justice of India.
STATE ADMINISTRATIVE TRIBUNALS (SATS)
  • 1. Establishment: The Administrative Tribunals Act of 1985 empowers the Central government to establish SATs at the specific request of the concerned state governments.
  • 2. Original Jurisdiction: The SATs exercise original jurisdiction in relation to recruitment and all service matters of state government employees.
  • 3. Appointment: The chairman and members of the SATs are appointed by the President after consultation with the Governor of the state concerned.
JOINT ADMINISTRATIVE TRIBUNAL (JAT)
  • 1. Provision: The Administrative Tribunals Act of 1985 also makes a provision for setting up Joint Administrative Tribunal (JAT) for two or more states.
  • 2. Powers: A JAT exercises all the jurisdiction and powers exercisable by the administrative tribunals for such states.
  • 3. Appointment: The chairman and members of a JAT are appointed by the President after consultation with the Governors of the concerned states.

CH19- Election Commission

Part XV — Article 324 - 329

ARTICLE 324

Scope: Superintendence, direction and control of elections to be vested in an Election Commission.

  • 1. Parliament Elections: All elections to Parliament.
  • 2. State Legislature: All elections to the Legislature of every State.
  • 3. President & VP: Elections to the offices of President and Vice-President.

Composition: (2) Election Commission shall consist of the Chief Election Commissioner and two Election Commissioners,

Chairman: (3) The Chief Election Commissioner shall act as the Chairman of the Election Commission.

Regional Commissioners: (4) Before each general election to the House of the People and to the Legislative Assembly of each State, and each biennial election to the Legislative Council of each State having such Council, the President may also appoint after consultation with the Election Commission such Regional Commissioners as he may consider necessary to assist the Election Commission.

CEC Removal: (5) The Chief Election Commissioner shall not be removed from his office except in like manner and on the like grounds as a Judge of the Supreme Court and the conditions of service of the Chief Election Commissioner shall not be varied to his disadvantage after his appointment.

EC Removal: Election Commissioner or a Regional Commissioner shall not be removed from office except on the recommendation of the Chief Election Commissioner.

ARTICLE 325

Electoral Roll: There shall be one general electoral roll for every territorial constituency for election to either House of Parliament or to the House or either House of the Legislature of a State.

No Discrimination: No person to be ineligible for inclusion in, or to claim to be included in a special, electoral roll on grounds of religion, race, caste or sex.

ARTICLE 326

Adult Suffrage: Elections to the House of the People and to the Legislative Assemblies of States to be on the basis of adult suffrage.

ARTICLE 327

Parliament Power: Power of Parliament to make provision with respect to elections to Legislatures.

ARTICLE 328

State Legislature Power: Power of Legislature of a State to make provision with respect to elections to such Legislature.

MODEL CODE OF CONDUCT
  • 1. Definition: The MCC is a set of guidelines issued by the Election Commission to regulate political parties and candidates prior to elections, to ensure free and fair elections.
  • 2. Duration: The MCC is operational from the date that the election schedule is announced till the date that results are announced.
  • 3. Origin: The MCC was first introduced in the state assembly elections in Kerala in 1960.
  • 4. Manifestos: In 2013, the Supreme Court directed the Election Commission to include guidelines regarding election manifestos.
  • 5. No Statute: The MCC has no statutory backing.
ELECTION SILENCE
  • 1. Prohibition: Holding public meetings during the 48-hour period before the hour fixed for the closing of the poll is prohibited.
  • 2. Definition: The 48-hour period is known as election silence.
THE EXPENDITURE LIMIT
  • 1. Lok Sabha Limit: A candidate can spend between Rs 50 lakh and Rs 70 lakh, depending on the state they are contesting the Lok Sabha election from.
  • 2. General Cap: For all states, except Arunachal Pradesh, Goa and Sikkim, a candidate can spend a maximum of Rs 70 lakh on canvassing.
  • 3. Small State Cap: The cap for Arunachal Pradesh, Goa and Sikkim is Rs 54 lakh.
  • 4. Delhi & UTs: And, it is Rs 70 lakh for Delhi and Rs 54 lakh for other Union territories.
  • 5. Assembly Limit: For the assembly elections, the ceiling is between Rs 20 lakh and Rs 28 lakh.

POLITICAL PARTIES
  • 1. Multi-Party System: India follows multi-party system and has the largest number of political parties in the world.
  • 2. Registration: Registration of Political parties is governed by the provisions of Section 29A of the Representation of the People Act, 1951.
NATIONAL POLITICAL PARTY OF INDIA
  • 1. Valid Votes: It secures at least six percent of the valid votes polled in any four or more states, at a general election to the House of the People or, to the State Legislative Assembly.
  • 2. Four Seats: In addition, it wins at least four seats in the House of the People from any State or States.
  • 3. Two Percent Seats: It wins at least two percent seats in the House of the People (i.e., 11 seats in the existing House having 543 members), and these members are elected from at least three different States.
  • 4. National Parties: Bahujan Samaj Party, Bhartiya Janta Party, Indian National Congress, Communist Party of India, Communist Party of India (Marxist), National Congress Party.
STATE POLITICAL PARTY
  • 1. Six Percent Votes: It secures at least six percent of the valid votes polled in the State at a general election, either to the House of the People or to the Legislative Assembly of the State concerned.
  • 2. Two Assembly Seats: In addition, it wins at least two seats in the Legislative Assembly of the State concerned.
  • 3. Three Percent Seats: It wins at least three percent (3%) of the total number of seats in the Legislative Assembly of the State, or at least three seats in the Assembly, whichever is more.

ISSUES
  • 1. Commissioner Appointment: The government continues to appoint election commissioners on its own.
  • 2. Removal Process: Removal of the Election Commissioners does not require such a process of impeachment.
  • 3. Financial Dependence: It is still dependent on the Union Government for financial matters.
  • 4. MCC Enforcement: There is no clarity regarding the enforcing the Model Code of Conduct.
  • 5. No Legal Backing: ✓ MCC has not been given any legal backing.
  • 6. Deregistration Power: EC cannot deregister political parties in any event, for the gravest of infringement.
  • 7. Inner-Party Democracy: The ECI has no power in enforcing inner-party democracy and regulation of party finances.
  • 8. Disqualification Power: The EC does not have the power to disqualify candidates who commit electoral malpractices.
SUGGESTIONS
  • 1. CEC Appointment: The CEC should be appointed by the President in consultation with the Chief Justice of India and the Leader of the Opposition in the Lok Sabha. (Goswami Committee)
  • 2. Equal Procedures: There must be similar election and removal procedures for CEC and ECs, and they must exercise the same powers unless specifically prescribed by law.
  • 3. Charged Expenses: The expenses of ECI must be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India.
  • 4. Independent Secretariat: Create a permanent, independent secretariat.

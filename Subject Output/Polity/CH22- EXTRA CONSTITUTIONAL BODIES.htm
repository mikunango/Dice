Extra Constitutional Bodies

LOKPAL

1. Anti-Corruption Ombudsman: The idea of creating an anti corruption ombudsman, in the form of a Lokpal, was first conceptualized in 1968 in the fourth Lok Sabha.

2. Legislative Efforts: Thereafter in 1971, 1977, 1985, 1989, 1996, 1998 and 2001 efforts were made to enact legislation to create the institution of Lokpal.

  • 1. First Lokpal Appointed: Retired Supreme Court judge Pinaki Chandra Ghose is appointed as the first Lokpal of India
PURPOSE

1. Corruption Inquiry Mandate: It is an institution that will inquire into allegations of corruption against certain public functionaries.

SELECTION
  • 1. Search Committee Process: Under Rule 11 (2) of the Search Committee Rules, 2014, the search panel for lokpal recommends at least five names for chairperson and at least three times the number of vacancies in the case of members.
  • 2. Selection Committee: This recommendation has to be placed before the high-power selection committee led by the Prime Minister.
  • 3. Committee Composition: The selection committee comprises the Lok Sabha Speaker, the Leader of the Opposition, the Chief Justice of India and an eminent jurist as members.

3. SC Urgency: The court has for the past several months been constantly urging the government to complete the Lokpal appointment.

  • 4. Implementation Delay: Although passed in 2014, the Lokpal and Lokayukta Act of 2013 was not implemented all these years because there was no Leader of Opposition (LoP) in the 16th Lok Sabha.
  • 5. SC Clarification: The Supreme Court clarified that the Lokpal appointment process need not be stalled merely due to the absence of the LoP.
  • 6. Statutory Provision: For this, the court points to sub-section (2) of Section 4 of the original 2013 Lokpal Act.
  • 7. Validity Safeguard: The section makes it clear that the appointment of the chairperson or members of Lokpal will not be invalidated merely because one of the members of the selection committee (the LoP) is missing.
Page 215
  • 8. Available Members Suffice: In short, the available members of the Lokpal selection committee could very well recommend suitable persons to the President for appointment to Lokpal.
  • 9. Workable Legislation: Thus, the Lokpal Act 2013 is deemed to be an eminently workable piece of legislation by the Supreme court.
  • 10. Standing Committee Report: Also, a Parliamentary Standing Committee submitted its report on December 3, 2015, fully supporting the amendment to replace the LoP with the single largest Opposition party leader in the Lok Sabha.
STRUCTURE
  • 1. Statutory Body: The institution of Lokpal is a statutory body without any constitutional backing.
  • 2. Multi-Member Body: Lokpal is a multi-member body, made up of one chairperson and maximum of 8 members.

1. Chairperson Qualification: The person who is to be appointed as the chairperson of the Lokpal should be either the former Chief Justice of India or a former Judge of the Supreme Court, or an eminent person with impeccable integrity and outstanding ability, having special knowledge and expertise of minimum 25 years in the matters relating to anti-corruption policy, public administration, vigilance, finance including insurance and banking, law and management.

2. Judicial Composition: Out of the maximum eight members, half will be judicial members.

  • 3. Reservation Mandate: Minimum fifty per cent of the Members will be from SC / ST / OBC / Minorities and women.
  • 4. Judicial Member: The JUDICIAL MEMBER of the Lokpal should be either a former Judge of the Supreme Court or a former Chief Justice of a High Court.
  • 5. Non-Judicial Member: The NON-JUDICIAL member should be an eminent person with impeccable integrity and outstanding ability, having special knowledge and expertise of a minimum of 25 years in the matters relating to anti-corruption policy, public administration, vigilance, finance including insurance and banking, law and management.

3. Appointment Mechanism: The members are appointed by the president on the recommendation of a selection committee.

4. Selection Committee: The selection committee is composed of the Prime Minister who is the Chairperson; Speaker of Lok Sabha, Leader of Opposition in Lok Sabha, Chief Justice of India or a Judge nominated by him/her, and one eminent jurist.

ROLE
  • 1. Anti-Corruption Ombudsman: The Lokpal is an anti-corruption Ombudsman.
  • 2. Jurisdiction Over Executives: The Lokpal will have its jurisdiction over top political executives, including the Prime Minister.
  • 3. Direction Power: They have the powers to give directions to all the agencies in India, like the CBI, the CVC, etc.
  • 4. Suspend or Transfer: They can suspend or transfer any officer who is coming in the way of an enquiry.
  • 5. Investigative and Prosecuting: The Lokpal will work as an investigative agency, as well as a prosecuting agency.
  • 6. Special Courts: They shall have special courts.

1. ARC Recommendation: Even the 1966 Administrative Reforms Commission, headed by Morarji Desai had suggested various steps to check graft in political circles and official circles.

NEED
  • 1. Credibility Erosion: All executive institutions are losing credibility.
  • 2. Judicial Encroachment: The judiciary has encroached.
  • 3. Executive Authority Eroded: The power and authority of the executive has been significantly eroded.
  • 4. No Investigating Agency: The irony is that the Lokpal doesn't have an investigating agency of its own; it would have to depend upon the CBI.
THE ISSUES INVOLVED IN THE FUNCTIONING OF LOKPAL AND METHODS TO MAKE LOKPAL EFFECTIVE

1. Lokpal Functioning: The Lokpal, a multi-member body, made up of one chairperson and maximum of 8 members was enacted in 2013 and came into force in 2014. The Lokpal has attempted to make a positive change but still, there are loopholes and inconsistencies that need to be addressed.

  • 1. Skewed Selection Process: The Lokpal selection process is skewed because there are no guidelines for deciding who is a "eminent jurist" or "a person of integrity."
  • 2. Political Influence: In addition to appointing members from political parties, the Lokpal committee itself is subject to political influence.
  • 3. Limited PM Inquiry: The Act prohibits Lokpal inquiry if the allegations against the PM in certain circumstances. Thus, Lokpal do not have full authority to investigate PM.
    • a. Two-Thirds Approval: Also, complaints against the PM are not to be probed unless the full Lokpal bench considers the initiation of an inquiry and at least two-thirds of the members approve it.
  • 4. CBI Dependence: All the cases of corruption in which high officials are involved go to the CBI. Lokpal do not have complete control.
SUGGESTION
  • 1. Independent Mechanism: Need for a mechanism that provides for simple, independent, speedy means of delivering justice without succumbing to the clutches of the executive.
  • 2. Functional Autonomy: It must be strengthened in respect of functional autonomy and workforce availability.
  • 3. Eliminate Root Causes: The government should focus on eliminating the root causes because of which the general public is demanding a Lokpal.
  • 4. Transparent Nomination: There should be complete transparency when nominating a Lokpal as it will increase the chances for the right person to be appointed.

1. Independent Functioning: Lokpal shall function independently of any political influence so that a proper system of checks-and-balance is maintained in the federal and democratic system of India.

CENTRAL BUREAU OF INVESTIGATION

1. CBI Establishment: The Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI) is a premier investigating police agency in India, and is an elite force which plays a major role in preservation of values in public life and in ensuring the health of the national economy. The agency was established in 1941 as the Special Police Establishment, and was tasked with simple domestic security outlining. The Central Bureau of Investigation was later established on 1 April 1963(Santhanam Committee). Its motto is "INDUSTRY, IMPARTIALITY, AND INTEGRITY".

2. Non-Statutory Status: The CBI is not a statutory body. It derives its powers from the Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946. It works under the overall superintendence of Central Vigilance Commission in matters related to the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988.

COMPOSITION
  • 1. Director and Officers: The CBI is headed by a Director. He is assisted by a Special Director or an Additional Director. It has a number of joint directors, deputy inspector generals, superintendents of police and all other usual ranks of police personnel.
  • 2. Tenure Security: The Director of CBI as Inspector-General of Police. The Director of CBI has been provided security of two-year tenure in office by the CVC Act, 2003 (Vineet Narain Case). The CVC Act also provides the mechanism for the selection of the Director of CBI and other officers of the rank of SP and above in the CBI.
  • 3. Appointment Process: The Director of the CBI is appointed by the Central Government on the recommendation of a committee consisting of the Central Vigilance Commissioner as Chairperson, the Vigilance Commissioners, the Secretary to the Government of India in-charge of the Ministry of Home Affairs and the Secretary (Coordination and Public Grievances) in the Cabinet Secretariat.
ORGANIZATION

1. CBI Divisions: The CBI has the following divisions:

  • a. Anti-Corruption Division
  • b. Economic Offences Division
  • c. Special Crimes Division
  • d. Policy and International Police Cooperation Division
  • e. Administration Division
  • f. Directorate of Prosecution
  • g. Central Forensic Science Laboratory
FUNCTIONS

1. Investigation Divisions: CBI has grown into a multidisciplinary investigation agency over a period of time. Today it has the following three divisions for investigation of crime:-

  • 1. Anti-Corruption Cases: Investigation of cases under the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 against Public officials and the employees of Central Government, Public Sector Undertakings, Corporations or Bodies owned or controlled by the Government of India.
  • 2. Economic Fraud Investigation: Investigation of major financial scams and serious economic frauds, including crimes relating to Fake Indian Currency Notes, Bank Frauds and Cyber Crime.
  • 3. Serious Crime Investigation: Investigation of serious, sensational and organized crime under the Indian Penal Code and other laws on the requests of State Governments or on the orders of the Supreme Court

1. Investigation Authority: and High Courts. The laws under which CBI can investigate Crime are notified by the Central Government under section 3 of the DSPE Act.

2. CBI Takeover Conditions: CBI take over the investigation of a criminal case registered by the State Police

  • 1. State Government Request: The concerned State Government makes a request to that effect and the Central Government agrees to it
  • 2. Consent Notification: The State Government issues notification of consent under section 6 of the DSPE Act and the Central Government issues notification under section 5 of the DSPE Act
  • 3. Court Orders: The Supreme Court or High Courts orders CBI to take up such investigations.

3. Suo-Moto Jurisdiction: As per section 2 of the DSPE Act, CBI can suo-moto take up investigation of offences notified in section 3 only in the Union Territories.

4. State Consent Requirement: Taking up investigation by CBI in the boundaries of a State requires prior consent of that State as per Section 6 of the DSPE Act.

5. Central Authorization: The Central Government can authorize CBI to investigate such a crime in a State but only with the consent of the concerned State Government.

6. Organized Crime Investigation: Investigating serious crimes, having national and international ramifications, committed by organized gangs of professional criminals.

7. Crime Statistics: Maintaining crime statistics and disseminating criminal information.

8. Interpol Function: The CBI acts as the "National Central Bureau" of Interpol in India. The Interpol Wing of the CBI coordinates requests for investigation-related activities originating from Indian law enforcement agencies and the member countries of the Interpol.

CBI-STILL CAGED PARROT

1. SC Criticism: Supreme Court of India has made several observation and comment against the inefficient functioning of the CBI and still this is continuing without much improving the situation. The Supreme Court of India has criticised the CBI by calling it a "caged parrot speaking in its master's voice".

  • 1. Executive Superintendence: It functions under the superintendence of the Deptt. of Personnel, Ministry of Personnel, Pension & Public Grievances, Government of India — which falls under the prime minister's office.
  • 2. Non-Statutory Status: The CBI is not a statutory body but derives its power to investigate from the Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946.
  • 3. Political Misuse: It has often been used by the government of the day to cover up wrongdoing, keep coalition allies in line and political opponents at bay.
  • 4. Autonomy Deficit: Lack of enough financial and administrative autonomy in non-corruption cases has been often cited as major roadblocks in independent investigation by CBI.
  • 5. Staffing Dependence: Dependence on MHA for staffing, especially on deputations of IPS officers, which affects its functional autonomy.
  • 6. RTI Exemption: CBI is exempted from the provisions of the Right to Information Act, thus, lacking public accountability.
Page 219
  • 1. Prior Approval Obstacle: Prior approval of Central Government to conduct inquiry or investigation on the employees of the Central Government, of the level of Joint Secretary and above is a big obstacle in combating corruption at higher levels of bureaucracy.

1. Statutory Status Need: As long as the government of the day has the power to transfer and post officials of its choice in the CBI, the investigating agency will not enjoy autonomy and will be unable to investigate cases freely. Providing statutory status through legislation equivalent to that provided to the Comptroller & Auditor General and the Election Commission will help maintain the independence of the institution.

CENTRAL INFORMATION COMMISSION

1. Statutory Establishment: The Central Information Commission set up under the Right to Information Act is the authorized quasi judicial body, established in 2005.

  • 1. Commission Composition: It has chief information commissioner and up to 10 information commissioners.
    • a. Appointment Process: All are appointed by the President on recommendation of Prime minister, union minister nominated by him and leader of opposition in Lok Sabha.
  • 2. Membership Qualification: Qualification for membership to commission are People should person of eminence in public life with experience in field of law, science and technology, governance, social service, management, journalism, mass media or administration.
    • a. Disqualification Criteria: They should not be MP / MLA's or connected to any political party, doing some business or holding office of profit.
  • 3. Tenure of Office: They hold office till age of 65 or 5 years.
    • a. Re-appointment Clause: The information commissioner is eligible for post of chief information commissioner but can be in office for maximum 5 years including his tenure of information commissioner.
  • 4. Removal Grounds: Removal is done by president on grounds of bankruptcy, unsound mind, infirmity of body or mind, sentenced to imprisonment for a crime, or engages in paid employment.
    • a. SC Inquiry Removal: He can also be removed for proved misbehavior or incapacity if SC inquiry finds him guilty. They can resign by writing to president.

  • (c) Civil Court Powers: While inquiring, the Commission has the powers of a civil court in respect of the following matters :
  • (d) Record Examination: During the inquiry of a complaint, the Commission may examine any record which is under the control of the public authority and no such record may be withheld from it on any grounds. In other words, all public records must be given to the Commission during inquiry for examination.
  • (e) Compliance Power: The Commission has the power to secure compliance of its decisions from the public authority.

  • 1. Summoning Power: Summoning and enforcing attendance of persons, compelling them to give oral or written evidence on oath and to produce documents or things;
  • 2. Document Inspection: Requiring the discovery and inspection of documents;

CENTRAL VIGILANCE COMMISSION

1. Statutory Origin: It is the apex vigilance institution, created on the recommendations of Santhanam committee in 1964 but was conferred with statutory status in 2003.

  • 1. Not Investigating Agency: It must be informed that Central Vigilance Commission is not an investigating agency.
  • 2. Coalition Functioning: It operates in coalition with the CBI or the Departmental Chief Vigilance Officers. It submits its report to the President of India.
  • 3. Commission Composition: The Commission shall consist of - A Central Vigilance Commissioner – Chairperson & Not more than two Vigilance Commissioners
  • 4. Appointment Process: They are appointed by the president on recommendations of a committee of PM + Home minister + leader of opposition Lok Sabha.
  • 5. Tenure of Office: They occupy post till age of 65 yrs or 4 year term.
  • 6. Post-Retirement Bar: They are not eligible for any other govt appointment under centre or state after ceasing to hold office.

2. Removal Grounds: They are removed by President on grounds of bankruptcy, unsound mind, infirmity of body or mind, sentenced to imprisonment for a crime, or engages in paid employment or has acquired financial or other interest that might affect his judgment.

  • 7. SC Inquiry Removal: He can also be removed for proved misbehaviour or incapacity if SC inquiry finds him guilty.
  • 8. Resignation Process: They can resign by writing to president.
PURPOSE
  • 1. Anti-Corruption Mandate: The main purpose for which this important body had been established was to ensure all sorts of corruptions in government sector could be well prevented and addressed minutely.
  • 2. Autonomous Vigilance Body: It is an autonomous body, responsible for monitoring all vigilance activities under the union government.
  • 3. Advisory Role: Its major role is to recommend government agencies in "planning, executing, reviewing and reforming" their vigilance capability.
  • 4. CVC Formation: Central Government of India formed CVC in the year 1964 as an important body that could take into account the measures and steps to prevent all the corruptions especially the governmental ones for a better system and governance.

FUNCTIONS
  • 1. Inquiry Power: To undertake an inquiry or cause an inquiry or investigation to be made into any transaction in which a public servant working in any organization, to which the executive control of the Government of India extends, is suspected or alleged to have acted for an improper purpose or in a corrupt manner;
  • 2. Disciplinary Advisory: To tender independent and impartial advice to the disciplinary and other authorities in disciplinary cases, involving vigilance angle at different stages i.e. investigation, inquiry, appeal, review etc.
  • 3. Supervisory Check: To exercise a general check and supervision over vigilance and anti-corruption work in Ministries or Departments of the Government of India and other organizations to which the executive power of the Union extends; and
  • 4. Whistleblower Protection: To undertake or cause an inquiry into complaints received under the Public Interest Disclosure and Protection of Informer and recommend appropriate action.
  • 5. Consultation Mandate: The Central Government is required to consult the CVC in making rules and regulations governing the vigilance and disciplinary matters relating to the members of Central Services and All India Services.
  • 6. DSPE Superintendence: To exercise superintendence over the functioning of the Delhi Special Police Establishment with respect to investigation under the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988; or offence under CrPC for certain categories of public servants.
  • 7. DSPE Investigation Review: To give directions and to review the progress of investigations conducted by the DSPE.
  • 8. CBI Director Appointment: The Director of the CBI and Director of Enforcement should be appointed on the recommendations of a Committee headed by the Central Vigilance Commissioner, the Home Secretary and the Secretary in the Department of Personnel (Vineet Narain case).
LIMITATIONS
  • 1. Advisory Body Only: CVC is only an advisory body. Central Government Departments are free to either accept or reject CVC's advice in corruption cases.
  • 2. Inadequate Resources: CVC does not have adequate resources compared with number of complaints that it receives. It is a very small set up with sanctioned staff strength of 299. Whereas, it is supposed to check corruption in more than 1500 central government departments and ministries.
  • 3. Joint Secretary Restriction: CVC cannot direct CBI to initiate inquiries against any officer of the level of Joint Secretary and above on its own. Such permission has to be obtained from the concerned department.
  • 4. No Criminal Power: CVC does not have powers to register criminal case. It deals only with vigilance or disciplinary cases.
  • 5. Limited CBI Oversight: CVC has supervisory powers over CBI. However, CVC does not have the power to call for any file from CBI or to direct CBI to investigate any case in a particular manner. CBI is under administrative control of Department of Personnel and Training (DoPT). Which means that, the powers to appoint, transfer, suspend CBI officers lie with DoPT.

NATIONAL HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISSION

1. UDHR Origin: The UN Commission on Human Rights framed the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR). UDHR was approved by the United Nations General Assembly in 1948.

  • 1. Customary Law Force: Although the UDHR was a non-binding resolution, it is now reflected to have acquired the force of international customary which may be raised in appropriate circumstances by national and other judges.
  • 2. UDHR Objectives: The UDHR desires member nations to promote a number of human, civil, economic and social rights.
  • 3. Human Rights Day: The implementation of the Universal Declaration is a significant international commemoration marked each year on 10 December, and is called Human Rights Day or International Human Rights Day.
  • 4. Institutional Strengthening: The international community has acknowledged the mounting importance of strengthening national human rights institutions.
  • 5. Paris Principles: In this background, in the year 1991, a UN-sponsored meeting of representatives of national institutions held in Paris, a comprehensive set of principles on the status of national institutions was developed, these are commonly known as the Paris Principles.

2. Foundation Basis: These principles, became the basis for the establishment and operation of national human rights institutions.

  • 6. NHRC Establishment: The National Human Rights Commission, abbreviated as NHRC of India is an autonomous public body founded on 12 October 1993 under the Protection of Human Rights Ordinance of 28 September 1993.
  • 7. Statutory Base: It was given a statutory base by the Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993 (TPHRA).

3. Civil Court Powers: NHRC has the powers of a civil court. It has authority to grant interim relief. It has authority to recommend payment of compensation or damages.

  • 8. Implementation Monitoring: It has exclusive mechanism with which it also monitors implementation of its various recommendations.
EXECUTIVES
  • 1. Chairperson: A Chairperson, retired Chief Justice of India.
  • 2. SC Judge Member: One Member who is, or has been, a Judge of the Supreme Court of India.
  • 3. HC Chief Justice Member: One Member who is, or has been, the Chief Justice of a High Court.
Page 224
  • 1. HR Members: Two Members to be appointed from among persons having knowledge of, or practical experience in, matters relating to human rights.
    • a. Ex Officio Members: Additionally, the Chairpersons of four National members (1.Minorities 2.SC and ST 3.Women), to serve as ex officio members.
  • 2. Appointment Process: The Chairperson and the Members of the Commission are appointed by the President on the recommendations of a Committee consisting of the Prime Minister as Chairperson.
  • 3. Committee Members: The members are Speaker of the House of the People, Union Minister of Home Affairs, Leader of the Opposition in the House of the People, Leader of the Opposition in the Council of States, Deputy Chairman of the Council of States.
  • 4. Tenure: Term of the Chairman and members is 5 years or 70 years of age whichever is earlier.
  • 5. Post-Retirement Bar: After retirement they are not eligible for further appointment under the Government of India or under the Government of any State.
  • 6. Removal Grounds: The Chairperson and members of NHRC can be removed only on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity.
  • 7. Removal Procedure: The President has to refer the matter to Supreme Court and if after inquiry Supreme Court finds that the charge is right; they can be removed by the President.

1. NHRC Headquarters: The headquarters of the commission is at New Delhi.

  • 8. Branch Offices: Though, with the permission from the government, it can establish offices at other places in India.
FUNCTIONS
  • 1. Grievance Investigation: To investigate grievances regarding the violation of human rights either suo moto or after receiving a petition.
  • 2. Scrutiny of Officials: To scrutinize the failure of duties on the part of any public official in preventing the violation of human rights.
  • 3. Judicial Intervention: To interfere in any judicial proceedings involving any allegation of violation of human rights.
  • 4. Jail Inspection: To visit any jail or any other institution under the control of the State Government to see the living conditions of the inmates and to make recommendations thereon.
  • 5. Safeguard Review: To review the safeguards provided under the constitution or any law for the protection of the human rights and to recommend appropriate remedial measures.
  • 6. Treaty Study: To study treaties and other international instruments on human rights and to make recommendations for their effective implementation.
  • 7. Research Promotion: To undertake and promote research in the field of human rights.
  • 8. NGO Encouragement: To encourage the efforts of the non-governmental organisations working in the field of human rights.
  • 9. HR Literacy: To spread human rights literacy among various sections of society and to promote awareness of the safeguards available for the protection of these rights through publications, the media, seminars and other means.
Page 225
  • 10. Counter-Terrorism Review: To review all facts related to the activities of the terrorists which obstruct the way of the protection of human rights and to make recommendations for their effective implementation.
  • 11. Civil Court Powers: To make an inquiry into the complaints submitted to it, the commission has the powers of a civil court.

1. State HR Commission: The Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993, authorized the State Governments to establish their own commission for this aim. The chairman and the members of such State Commission are appointed by the Governor in consultation with the Chief Minister, Home Minister, Speaker and Leader of the Opposition in the State Legislative Assembly.

LIMITATIONS
  • 1. Recommendatory Nature: National Human Rights Commission can only make recommendations, without the power to impose decisions. This lack of authority to ensure compliance can lead to outright denial of its decision too.
  • 2. Bureaucratic Challenges: It is often visualized as a post-retirement destinations for judges, police officers and bureaucrats with political clout. Bureaucratic functioning, insufficiency of funds also hamper the working of the commission.
  • 3. One-Year Limitation: Under the Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993, human rights commissions cannot examine an event if the complaint was made more than one year after the incident. Therefore, a large number of genuine complaints go unaddressed.

2. NHRC Summary: To summarize, the Government of India did comprehend the need to establish an independent body to promote and protect human rights.

3. Autonomous Mandate: The establishment of an autonomous National Human Rights Commission by the Government of India mirrors its commitment for effective execution of human rights provisions under national and international instruments.

4. Accountability Linkage: According to news reports, Human rights commissions are most active when their tasks are passably supported by other mechanisms that safeguard a government's accountability.

NATIONAL COMMISSION FOR WOMEN
  • 1. Constitutional Norm: The norm of gender equality is protected in the Indian Constitution.
  • 2. Preamble Guarantee: The Preamble, promotes "Equality of status and of opportunity".
  • 3. FR and DPSP: The Fundamental Rights enshrined in Part III of the Indian Constitution and Directive Principles enshrined in Part IV of the Constitution all promote gender equality.
  • 4. Special Provisions: The Constitution not only allows equality to women but has also made special provisions for safeguarding equality.
  • 5. NCW Establishment: Thus in January 1992, the National Commission for Women was set up as a statutory body under the National Commission for Women Act, 1990 to perform the mandate set by the Act.
COMPOSITION

Five Members: Five Members to be nominated by the Central Government from amongst persons of ability, integrity and standing who have had experience in law or legislation, trade unionism, management of an industry potential of women, women's voluntary organizations ( including women activist ), administration, economic development, health, education or social welfare.

SIGNIFICANCE
  • 1. Patriarchal Society: Women. India has conventionally been a male-controlled society and therefore women are visualized as a weak entity in Indian society.
  • 2. No Specific Provision: The Constitution does not contain any provision precisely made to favour women as such.
  • 3. Enabling Articles: Though Art. 15 (3), Art. 21 and Art. 14 are in favour of women.
  • 4. Judicial Interpretation: The Supreme Court through interpretive processes has tried to extend some protections to women.

5. Landmark Judgments: Through judgments in cases such as Bodhisattwa Gautam v. Subra Chakraborty and the Chairman Rly Board v. Chandrima Das case where rape was declared a monstrous crime, as well as the breakthrough judgment in Visakha v. State of Rajasthan.

  • 6. Need for NCW: The absence of constitutional mechanism, judicial capability and social interest formed the motivation and need for the creation of the National Commission for Women.

7. Historical Context: It is apparent from the prior mentioned conditions and problems that women in India, though in a better position than their ancestors, were handicapped to a great extent in the early 1990s and these handicaps and prejudices against Indian women prompted the Indian Government to establish the first National Commission for Women in 1992.

STATUTORY FUNCTIONS
THE PREVENTION OF MONEY LAUNDERING ACT, 2002 (PMLA)
  • 1. Nature of Law: It is a criminal law enacted to prevent money laundering and to provide for confiscation of property derived from, or involved in, money-laundering and for matters connected therewith or incidental thereto.
  • 2. ED Responsibility: ED has been given the responsibility to enforce the provisions of the PMLA by conducting investigation to trace the assets derived from proceeds of crime, to provisionally attach the property and to ensure prosecution of the offenders and confiscation of the property by the Special court.
THE FOREIGN EXCHANGE MANAGEMENT ACT, 1999 (FEMA)
Page 229
  • 1. Nature of Law: It is a civil law enacted to consolidate and amend the laws relating to facilitate external trade and payments and to promote the orderly development and maintenance of foreign exchange market in India.
  • 2. ED Responsibility: ED has been given the responsibility to conduct investigation into suspected contraventions of foreign exchange laws and regulations, to adjudicate and impose penalties on those adjudged to have contravened the law.
THE FUGITIVE ECONOMIC OFFENDERS ACT, 2018 (FEOA)
  • 1. Purpose: This law was enacted to deter economic offenders from evading the process of Indian law by remaining outside the jurisdiction of Indian courts.
  • 2. ED Mandate: It is a law whereby Directorate is mandated to attach the properties of the fugitive economic offenders who have escaped from the India warranting arrest and provide for the confiscation of their properties to the Central Government.
THE FOREIGN EXCHANGE REGULATION ACT, 1973 (FERA)
  • 1. Functions under FERA: The main functions under the repealed FERA are to adjudicate the Show Cause Notices issued under the the said Act upto 31.5.2002 for the alleged contraventions of the Act which may result in imposition of penalties and to pursue prosecutions launched under FERA in the concerned courts.
SPONSORING AGENCY UNDER COFEPOSA
  • 1. Preventive Detention Power: Under the Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974 (COFEPOSA), this Directorate is empowered to sponsor cases of preventive detention with regard to contraventions of FEMA.
JURISDICTION
  • 1. Scope of Action: The ED can take action against any person on which FEMA or PMLA act applies.
  • 2. Court Jurisdiction: Cases under FEMA may lie in civil courts where PMLA cases will lie in criminal courts.
  • 3. Public Servants: All the public servants come under the jurisdiction of the agency if they are involved in any offence related to money laundering.
    • a. No Suo Motu Power: ED can not take an action suo motu.
  • 4. Investigation Process: The ED will investigate the matter and may attach the property of an accused person and also make an arrest and start proceeding with the violation of the provisions of FEMA and PMLA act.

1. Adjudication Process: The matter will be resolved by way of adjudication by courts or PMLA courts.

CHALLENGES
  • 1. Political Misuse: It is often used as a political tool by the ruling party to target its opponents and critics.
  • 2. Selective Enforcement: The ED has been accused of selective and arbitrary enforcement of cases, harassment and intimidation of witnesses and accused, and leaking sensitive information to the media to influence public opinion.

  • 3. Independence Concern: It raised questions about the independence and impartiality of the ED and its susceptibility to political interference.
  • 4. Low Conviction Rate: According to a report, out of 41,000 cases registered by the ED between 2009 and 2019, only 8,000 were charge-sheeted and only 3,000 resulted in a conviction.
  • 5. Low Success Rate: The ED has a low success rate in proving its cases and securing justice for the victims of financial crimes.
  • 6. Resource Deficit: It lacks adequate resources and manpower to deal with the increasing complexity and volume of economic offences.
  • 7. Cross-border Challenges: The ED also faces challenges in obtaining cooperation from foreign jurisdictions, accessing digital evidence, and dealing with new forms of financial crimes such as cryptocurrency and cyber fraud.

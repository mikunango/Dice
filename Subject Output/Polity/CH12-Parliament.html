CH12 - Parliament

Articles 79-98 | Composition, Duration & Officers of Parliament

PARLIAMENT
ARTICLE 79

1. Constitution of Parliament: There shall be a Parliament for the Union which shall consist of the President and two Houses to be known respectively as the Council of States and the House of the People.

COMPOSITION OF THE COUNCIL OF STATES
ARTICLE 80
  • 1. Composition: (1) The Council of States shall consist of —
    • a. Nominated Members: (a) twelve members to be nominated by the President in accordance with the provisions of clause (3); and
    • b. State Representatives: (b) not more than two hundred and thirty-eight representatives of the States and of the Union territories.
  • 2. Fourth Schedule: (2) The allocation of seats in the Council of States to be filled by representatives of the States and of the Union territories shall be in accordance with the provisions in that behalf contained in the Fourth Schedule.
  • 3. Nominated Persons: (3) The members to be nominated by the President under sub-clause (a) of clause (1) shall consist of persons having special knowledge or practical experience in respect of such matters as the following, namely:— Literature, Science, Art and Social Service.
  • 4. Election Method: (4) The representatives of each State in the Council of States shall be elected by the elected members of the Legislative Assembly of the State in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote.
  • 5. UT Representatives: (5) The representatives of the Union territories in the Council of States shall be chosen in such manner as Parliament may by law prescribe.
COMPOSITION OF THE HOUSE OF THE PEOPLE
ARTICLE 81
  • 1. Composition: (1) Subject to the provisions of article 331 the House of the People shall consist of —
    • a. State Members: (a) not more than five hundred and thirty members chosen by direct election from territorial constituencies in the States, and
    • b. UT Members: (b) not more than twenty members to represent the Union territories, chosen in such manner as Parliament may by law provide.
  • 2. Seat Allocation: (2) For the purposes of sub-clause (a) of clause (1),—
Page 2
  • 1. Population Ratio: (a) there shall be allotted to each State a number of seats in the House of the People in such manner that the ratio between that number and the population of the State is, so far as practicable, the same for all States; and
  • 2. Constituency Division: (b) each State shall be divided into territorial constituencies in such manner that the ratio between the population of each constituency and the number of seats allotted to it is, so far as practicable, the same throughout the State:
  • 3. Population Definition: (3) In this article, the expression "population" means the population as ascertained at the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published:
  • 4. 84th Amendment Proviso: Provided that the reference in this clause to the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published shall, until the relevant figures for the first census taken after the year 2026 (Eighty-fourth Amendment) have been published, be construed —
    • a. 1971 Census Reference: (i) for the purposes of sub-clause (a) of clause (2) and the proviso to that clause, as a reference to the 1971 census; and
    • b. 2001 Census Reference: (ii) for the purposes of sub-clause (b) of clause (2) as a reference to the 2001 census.
READJUSTMENT AFTER EACH CENSUS
ARTICLE 82

1. Seat Readjustment: Upon the completion of each census, the allocation of seats in the House of the People to the States and the division of each State into territorial constituencies shall be readjusted by such authority and in such manner as Parliament may by law determine.

DURATION OF HOUSES OF PARLIAMENT
ARTICLE 83
  • 1. Council Continuity: (1) The Council of States shall not be subject to dissolution, but as nearly as possible one-third of the members thereof shall retire as soon as may be on the expiration of every second year in accordance with the provisions made in that behalf by Parliament by law.
  • 2. Lok Sabha Duration: (2) The House of the People, unless sooner dissolved, shall continue for five years from the date appointed for its first meeting and no longer and the expiration of the said period of five years shall operate as a dissolution of the House:
  • 3. Emergency Extension: Provided that the said period may, while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, be extended by Parliament by law for a period not exceeding one year at a time and not extending in any case beyond a period of six months after the Proclamation has ceased to operate.
QUALIFICATION FOR MEMBERSHIP OF PARLIAMENT
ARTICLE 84

1. Membership Qualification: A person shall not be qualified to be chosen to fill a seat in Parliament unless he —

  • 1. Citizenship & Oath: (a) is a citizen of India, and makes and subscribes before some person authorised in that behalf by the Election Commission an oath or affirmation according to the form set out for the purpose in the Third Schedule;
Page 3
  • 1. Age Qualification: (b) is, in the case of a seat in the Council of States, not less than thirty years of age and, in the case of a seat in the House of the People, not less than twenty-five years of age; and
  • 2. Other Qualifications: (c) possesses such other qualifications as may be prescribed in that behalf by or under any law made by Parliament.
SESSIONS OF PARLIAMENT, PROROGATION AND DISSOLUTION
ARTICLE 85
  • 1. Summoning Power: (1) The President shall from time to time summon each House of Parliament to meet at such time and place as he thinks fit, but six months shall not intervene between its last sitting in one session and the date appointed for its first sitting in the next session.
  • 2. Prorogation & Dissolution: (2) The President may from time to time —
    • a. Prorogation: (a) prorogue the Houses or either House;
    • b. Dissolution: (b) dissolve the House of the People.
RIGHTS OF MINISTERS AND ATTORNEY-GENERAL AS RESPECTS HOUSES
ARTICLE 88

1. Right to Participate: Every Minister and the Attorney-General of India shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, either House, any joint sitting of the Houses, and any committee of Parliament of which he may be named a member, but shall not by virtue of this article be entitled to vote.

THE CHAIRMAN AND DEPUTY CHAIRMAN OF THE COUNCIL OF STATES
ARTICLE 89
  • 1. Ex Officio Chairman: (1) The Vice-President of India shall be ex officio Chairman of the Council of States.
  • 2. Deputy Chairman: (2) The Council of States shall, as soon as may be, choose a member of the Council to be Deputy Chairman thereof and, so often as the office of Deputy Chairman becomes vacant, the Council shall choose another member to be Deputy Chairman thereof.
ARTICLE 90

1. Vacation of Office: A member holding office as Deputy Chairman of the Council of States —

  • 1. Cessation of Membership: (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the Council;
  • 2. Resignation Provision: (b) may at any time, by writing under his hand addressed to the Chairman, resign his office; and
  • 3. Removal by Resolution: (c) may be removed from his office by a resolution of the Council passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council:
  • 4. Notice Requirement: Provided that no resolution for the purpose of clause (c) shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution.
ARTICLE 91
  • 1. Vacancy Provision: (1) While the office of Chairman is vacant, or during any period when the Vice-President is acting as, or discharging the functions of, President, the duties of the office shall be performed by the
Page 4
  • 1. Deputy Chairman Role: Deputy Chairman, or, if the office of Deputy Chairman is also vacant, by such member of the Council of States as the President may appoint for the purpose.
  • 2. Absence of Chairman: (2) During the absence of the Chairman from any sitting of the Council of States the Deputy Chairman, or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the Council, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the Council, shall act as Chairman.
ARTICLE 92
  • 1. Non-Presiding During Removal: (1) At any sitting of the Council of States, while any resolution for the removal of the Vice-President from his office is under consideration, the Chairman, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Chairman from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Chairman, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 91 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Chairman, or, as the case may be, the Deputy Chairman, is absent.
  • 2. Right to Speak: (2) The Chairman shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Council of States while any resolution for the removal of the Vice-President from his office is under consideration in the Council, but, notwithstanding anything in article 100, shall not be entitled to vote at all on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings.
THE SPEAKER AND DEPUTY SPEAKER OF THE HOUSE OF THE PEOPLE
ARTICLE 93

1. Election of Speaker: The House of the People shall, as soon as may be, choose two members of the House to be respectively Speaker and Deputy Speaker thereof and, so often as the office of Speaker or Deputy Speaker becomes vacant, the House shall choose another member to be Speaker or Deputy Speaker, as the case may be.

ARTICLE 94

1. Vacation of Office: A member holding office as Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the House of the People —

  • 1. Cessation of Membership: (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the House of the People;
  • 2. Resignation Provision: (b) may at any time, by writing under his hand addressed, if such member is the Speaker, to the Deputy Speaker, and if such member is the Deputy Speaker, to the Speaker, resign his office; and
  • 3. Removal by Resolution: (c) may be removed from his office by a resolution of the House of the People passed by a majority of all the then members of the House:
  • 4. Notice Requirement: Provided that no resolution for the purpose of clause (c) shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution:
  • 5. Speaker Continuation: Provided further that, whenever the House of the People is dissolved, the Speaker shall not vacate his office until immediately before the first meeting of the House of the People after the dissolution.
ARTICLE 95
Page 5
  • 1. Speaker Vacancy: (1) While the office of Speaker is vacant, the duties of the office shall be performed by the Deputy Speaker or, if the office of Deputy Speaker is also vacant, by such member of the House of the People as the President may appoint for the purpose.
  • 2. Absence of Speaker: (2) During the absence of the Speaker from any sitting of the House of the People the Deputy Speaker or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the House, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the House, shall act as Speaker.
ARTICLE 96
  • 1. Non-Presiding During Removal: (1) At any sitting of the House of the People, while any resolution for the removal of the Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Speaker, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Speaker, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 95 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Speaker, or, as the case may be, the Deputy Speaker, is absent.
  • 2. Speaker's Voting Right: (2) The Speaker shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the House of the People while any resolution for his removal from office is under consideration in the House and shall, notwithstanding anything in article 100, be entitled to vote only in the first instance on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings but not in the case of an equality of votes.
SALARIES AND ALLOWANCES OF THE CHAIRMAN AND DEPUTY CHAIRMAN AND THE SPEAKER AND DEPUTY SPEAKER
ARTICLE 97

1. Salary Provision: There shall be paid to the Chairman and the Deputy Chairman of the Council of States, and to the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker of the House of the People, such salaries and allowances as may be respectively fixed by Parliament by law and, until provision in that behalf is so made, such salaries and allowances as are specified in the Second Schedule.

SECRETARIAT OF PARLIAMENT

  • 3. Recruitment Regulation: (2) Parliament may by law regulate the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of either House of Parliament.
  • 4. Presidential Interim Power: (3) Until provision is made by Parliament under clause (2), the President may, after consultation with the Speaker of the House of the People or the Chairman of the Council

Secretarial Staff Rules: of States, as the case may be, make rules regulating the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of the House of the People or the Council of States, and any rules so made shall have effect subject to the provisions of any law made under the said clause.
DISQUALIFICATIONS OF MEMBERS
ARTICLE 101
  • 1. Dual House Bar: (1) No person shall be a member of both Houses of Parliament and provision shall be made by Parliament by law for the vacation by a person who is chosen a member of both Houses of his seat in one House or the other.
  • 2. Parliament-State Bar: (2) No person shall be a member both of Parliament and of a House of the Legislature of a State and if a person is chosen a member both of Parliament and of a House of the Legislature of a State, then, at the expiration of such period as may be specified in rules made by the President, that person's seat in Parliament shall become vacant, unless he has previously resigned his seat in the Legislature of the State.
  • 3. Disqualification Vacancy: (3) If a member of either House of Parliament —
    • a. Disqualification Ground: (a) becomes subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in clause (1) or clause (2) of article 102, or
    • b. Resignation Ground: (b) resigns his seat by writing under his hand addressed to the Chairman or the Speaker, as the case may be, and his resignation is accepted by the Chairman or the Speaker, as the case may be, his seat shall thereupon become vacant:
  • 4. Resignation Proviso: Provided that in the case of any resignation referred to in sub-clause (b), if from information received or otherwise and after making such inquiry as he thinks fit, the Chairman or the Speaker, as the case may be, is satisfied that such resignation is not voluntary or genuine, he shall not accept such resignation.
  • 5. Sixty-Day Absence: (4) If for a period of sixty days a member of either House of Parliament is without permission of the House absent from all meetings thereof, the House may declare his seat vacant:
  • 6. Absence Proviso: Provided that in computing the said period of sixty days no account shall be taken of any period during which the House is prorogued or is adjourned for more than four consecutive days.
ARTICLE 102
  • 1. Disqualification Grounds: (1) A person shall be disqualified for being chosen as, and for being, a member of either House of Parliament —
    • a. Office of Profit: (a) if he holds any office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State, other than an office declared by Parliament by law not to disqualify its holder;
    • b. Unsound Mind: (b) if he is of unsound mind and stands so declared by a competent court;
    • c. Undischarged Insolvent: (c) if he is an undischarged insolvent;
Page 7
  • 1. Citizenship Disqualification:
    • d. Non-Citizen: (d) if he is not a citizen of India, or has voluntarily acquired the citizenship of a foreign State, or is under any acknowledgment of allegiance or adherence to a foreign State;
    • e. Parliamentary Law: (e) if he is so disqualified by or under any law made by Parliament.
  • 2. Tenth Schedule: (2) A person shall be disqualified for being a member of either House of Parliament if he is so disqualified under the Tenth Schedule.
ARTICLE 103
  • 1. Presidential Decision: (1) If any question arises as to whether a member of either House of Parliament has become subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in clause (1) of article 102, the question shall be referred for the decision of the President and his decision shall be final.
  • 2. Election Commission Opinion: (2) Before giving any decision on any such question, the President shall obtain the opinion of the Election Commission and shall act according to such opinion.
ARTICLE 104

1. Penalty Provision: If a person sits or votes as a member of either House of Parliament before he has complied with the requirements of article 99, or when he knows that he is not qualified or that he is disqualified for membership thereof, or that he is prohibited from so doing by the provisions of any law made by Parliament, he shall be liable in respect of each day on which he so sits or votes to a penalty of five hundred rupees to be recovered as a debt due to the Union.

POWERS, PRIVILEGES AND IMMUNITIES OF PARLIAMENT AND ITS MEMBERS
ARTICLE 105
  • 1. Freedom of Speech: (1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution and to the rules and standing orders regulating the procedure of Parliament, there shall be freedom of speech in Parliament.
  • 2. Immunity from Proceedings: (2) No member of Parliament shall be liable to any proceedings in any court in respect of anything said or any vote given by him in Parliament or any committee thereof, and no person shall be so liable in respect of the publication by or under the authority of either House of Parliament of any report, paper, votes or proceedings.
  • 3. Parliamentary Privileges: (3) In other respects, the powers, privileges and immunities of each House of Parliament, and of the members and the committees of each House, shall be such as may from time to time be defined by Parliament by law, and, until so defined, shall be those of that House and of its members and committees immediately before the coming into force of section 15 of the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978.
  • 4. Extended Application: (4) The provisions of clauses (1), (2) and (3) shall apply in relation to persons who by virtue of this Constitution have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings, of a House of Parliament or any committee thereof as they apply in relation to members of Parliament.

ROLE OF RAJYA SABHA
The role of the Upper House is merely to delay legislation which might be the outcome of passions of the moment until the passions have subsided. -- Gopalaswami Ayyangar
  • 1. Obstructionist Criticism: The role of Rajya Sabha has come under the scanner as an obstructionist house.
  • 2. Federal Nature: Asserting the federal nature of the Indian polity, Rajya Sabha ensures healthy bicameralism not only as a House for second thought but is also a guardian of a State's rights as a House of correction.
  • 3. Scrutiny Function: It provides more opportunities for scrutiny and creates an enabling atmosphere for securing greater executive accountability.
  • 4. Quality Decline: But during the last two decades, we are witnessing intellectuals and academics being replaced by politicians with dubious motives.
  • 5. Disruption Issue: Rajya Sabha is seeing shouting and slanging matches between political parties.
  • 6. Financial Cost: In the Upper House, where the ruling party does not have a majority, has already cost the exchequer several crores this year.
  • 7. Legislative Hindrance: The Upper House has become a hindrance to the speedy legislative process that the country urgently requires for economic growth and progress.
  • 8. Backdoor Entry: Rajya Sabha has become a heaven for losers in elections, crony capitalists, compromised journalists and party fundraisers.
  • 9. Backdoor Elections: Many parties are now using the Rajya Sabha as a backdoor to get members elected, most of whom will not be able to win a Lok Sabha election.
  • 10. Sobriety Element: Nevertheless, Rajya Sabha would introduce an element of sobriety and second thought besides lending voice to the constituent units in the legislative scheme of things.
PARLIAMENTARY CONTROL OVER EXECUTIVE

1. Parliamentary Surveillance: The Indian system represents a real fusion of the highest executive and legislative authorities. In a parliamentary polity, Parliament embodies the will of the people and it must, therefore be able to oversee the way in which public policy is carried out so as to ensure that it keeps in step with the objectives of socio-economic progress, efficient administration and the aspirations, of the people as a whole. This, in a nutshell, is the raison d'etre of parliamentary surveillance of administration.

2. Procedural Devices: The various procedural devices like the system of parliamentary Committees, Questions, Calling Attention, Half-an-Hour Discussion, etc. constitute very potent instruments for effecting parliamentary surveillance over administrative action.

Page 9
  • 1. Review Occasions: Significant occasions for review of administration are also provided by the discussions on the Motion of Thanks on the President's Address, the Budget demands and particular aspects of governmental policy or situations.
  • 2. Specific Matters: Specific matters may be discussed through motions on matters of urgent public importance, private members' resolutions and other substantive motions.
  • 3. Ordinance Oversight: Ordinances, proclamation of emergency etc. can be done only when parliament ratifies showing the parliamentary oversight on the executive.
  • 4. Tax Control: Parliament control over taxes, which cannot be imposed without the consent of the parliament.
  • 5. Collective Responsibility: The collective responsibility of the Council of Ministers to the popular House (Loksabha).
  • 6. Cut Motion: The Parliament's control over the Budget through cut motion.
  • 7. Checks and Balances: All these measures make for a system of checks and balances in place thereby preventing the domination of any branch of the government.
  • 8. Accountability Framework: The makers of our constitution made sure that each arm of power could be held accountable for its actions by the other, by this logic, the executive, is accountable to the legislature for all its actions involving the implementation of laws or policies.

MN Kaul referred to the speaker as the conscience and guardian of the House.

  • 8. Judicial Review: The Speaker is not answerable to any person in performing the roles assigned to him. This is in stark contrast with the American system. In the Kihoto Hollohan case, the SC declared that the speaker is subject to judicial review.

DIFFERENCES: PARLIAMENTARY SYSTEM IN INDIA AND THE UK
  • 1. Head of State: The Head of the State in India is elected while in UK the Head of the State enjoys a hereditary position.
  • 2. Parliamentary Sovereignty: Parliament is not supreme in India and enjoys limited and restricted powers due to a written Constitution, the federal system, judicial review and fundamental rights while in UK the doctrine of the sovereignty of Parliament is followed.
  • 3. PM Membership: In India, the prime minister may be a member of any of the two Houses of Parliament while in UK the Prime minister should be a member of the House of Commons.
  • 4. Legal Responsibility: Britain has the system of legal responsibility of the minister while India has no such system.
  • 5. Shadow Cabinet: Shadow cabinet is a unique institution of the British cabinet system. It is formed by the opposition party to balance the ruling cabinet and to prepare its members for future ministerial office while there is no such institution in India.
  • 6. Minister Eligibility: In India, anyone can hold the position of Minister at the pleasure of the president, with due qualification as per law, even if he is not a member of both the houses. But he has to get elected within six months to either of the houses while Ministers of Britain are invariably selected among the members of the parliament.
  • 7. Cabinet Mechanism: In India President can ask the Prime Minister to place a subject before the council of ministers if it is not discussed and decided by the Cabinet while in the UK cabinet serves as a unit before the Parliament and Sovereign. Its views are placed by the cabinet as a single whole before the Parliament and Sovereign as if they are views of one man.
  • 8. Federal Structure: Indian federalism is based on Quasi-federal and works on competitive federalism while all powers of the government are vested in the British Parliament, which is a sovereign body.
PARLIAMENT PROCEDURE
OATH - ARTICLE 99

1. Oath Requirement: Every member of either House of Parliament shall, before taking his seat, make and subscribe before the President, or some person appointed in that behalf by him, an oath or affirmation according to the form set out for the purpose in the Third Schedule.

VOTING - ARTICLE 100

1. Majority Voting: (1) Save as otherwise provided in this Constitution, all questions at any sitting of either House or joint sitting of the Houses shall be determined by a majority of votes of the members present and voting, other than the Speaker or person acting as Chairman or Speaker.

Page 11

1. Casting Vote: The Chairman or Speaker, or person acting as such, shall not vote in the first instance, but shall have and exercise a casting vote in the case of an equality of votes.

2. Validity of Proceedings: (2) Either House of Parliament shall have power to act notwithstanding any vacancy in the membership thereof, and any proceedings in Parliament shall be valid notwithstanding that it is discovered subsequently that some person who was not entitled so to do sat or voted or otherwise took part in the proceedings.

QUORUM
ARTICLE 100
  • 1. Quorum Provision: (3) Until Parliament by law otherwise provides, the quorum to constitute a meeting of either House of Parliament shall be one-tenth of the total number of members of the House.
  • 2. No Quorum Duty: (4) If at any time during a meeting of a House there is no quorum, it shall be the duty of the Chairman or Speaker, or person acting as such, either to adjourn the House or to suspend the meeting until there is a quorum.
QUESTION HOUR
  • 1. Question Hour Slot: The first hour of every parliamentary sitting is slotted for this. During this time, the members ask questions and the ministers usually give answers. The questions are of three kinds, namely, starred, unstarred and short notice.
  • 2. Starred Question: A starred question (distinguished by an asterisk) requires an oral answer and hence supplementary questions can follow.
  • 3. Unstarred Question: An unstarred question, on the other hand, requires a written answer and hence, supplementary questions cannot follow.
  • 4. Short Notice Question: A short notice question is one that is asked by giving a notice of less than ten days. It is answered orally.
RESOLUTION

1. Resolution Definition: Is a motion through which the House expresses its majority opinion on a matter of public interest. Member must give 15 days notice before bringing up such a notice.

MOTION

1. Motion Purpose: A matter of urgent public interest is sought to be discussed in the House.

MOTION OF THANKS
  • 1. Thanks Motion: The President address is followed by a motion of thanks moved in each House.
  • 2. Discussion Period: Generally, three days are allotted for the discussion on the Motion of Thanks.
  • 3. Amended Form: If any of the amendments are put forward and accepted then the Motion of Thanks is adopted in the amended form.
  • 4. Government Defeat: The Motion of Thanks must be passed in the House. Otherwise, it amounts to the defeat of the government.
Page 12
CALLING ATTENTION MOTION {RULE - 197}
  • 1. Motion Introduction: It is introduced in the Parliament by a member to call the attention of a minister to a matter of urgent public importance, and to seek an authoritative statement from him on that matter.
  • 2. Indian Innovation: Like the zero hour, it is also an Indian innovation in the parliamentary procedure and has been in existence since 1954.
  • 3. Rules of Procedure: Unlike the zero hour, it is mentioned in the Rules of Procedure.
PRIVILEGE MOTION
  • 1. Breach of Privilege: It is concerned with the breach of parliamentary privileges by a minister.
  • 2. Motion Grounds: It is moved by a member when he feels that a minister has committed a breach of privilege of the House or one or more of its members by withholding facts of a case or by giving wrong or distorted facts.
  • 3. Censure Purpose: Its purpose is to censure the concerned minister.
ADJOURNMENT MOTION
  • 1. Adjournment Purpose: When there is an urgent matter of public importance then a member may propose that the business of the house be adjourned for discussing that matter.
  • 2. Speaker Consent: This motion can be moved only with the consent of the Speaker.
  • 3. Discussion Time: Generally such motions are discussed in the afternoon at 4.00 p.m.
  • 4. Member Support: Needed support of 50 members.
  • 5. Rajya Sabha Bar: Can't be passed in Rajya Sabha.
SHORT DISCUSSION
  • 1. Two-Hour Discussion: It is also known as two-hour discussion as the time allotted for such a discussion should not exceed two hours.
  • 2. Speaker Allotment: The Speaker can allot two days in a week for such discussions. There is neither a formal motion before the house nor voting.
  • 3. Existence Since 1953: This device has been in existence since 1953.
SUBSTANTIVE MOTION

1. Substantive Motion: A substantive motion is a self-contained independent proposal submitted for the approval of the House and drafted in such a way as to be capable of expressing a decision of the House, e.g., all resolutions are substantive motions.

SUBSIDIARY MOTION
  • 1. Subsidiary Nature: It is a motion which depends upon or relates to another motion or follows upon some proceedings in the House.
  • 2. Dependent Meaning: By itself it has no meaning and is not capable of stating the decision of the House without reference to the original motion or proceedings of the House.
Page 13
  • 1. Subsidiary Types: Subsidiary Motions are further divided into: (a) Ancillary Motion (b) Superseding Motion (c) Amendment.
NO-CONFIDENCE MOTION
  • 1. Lok Sabha Only: This can only be moved in Lok Sabha and the opposition of the house.
  • 2. No Specific Ground: No-confidence motion does not require any specific ground.
  • 3. Fifty Members: The motion needs the support of 50 members to be admitted.
  • 4. Ministers Resign: If it is passed, the Council of Ministers has to resign.
CENSURE MOTION
  • 1. Motion Scope: Can be moved against the ruling government or against any minister for the failure to act or seeking disapproval of their policy.
  • 2. Opposition Moved: Moved only in Lok Sabha by the opposition.
  • 3. Specific Charges: Must specify the charges against the government for which it is moved.
  • 4. Confidence Requirement: If a censure motion is passed in the Lok Sabha, the Council of ministers is bound to seek the confidence of the Lok Sabha as early as possible.
MOTIONS AND RESOLUTIONS DIFFERENT FROM EACH OTHER

1. Procedural Devices: Motions and resolutions are procedural devices to raise a discussion in the House on a matter of general public interest. With few exceptions, the process of debate in the House is initiated by a member or Minister by making a motion.

MOTION
  • 1. Motion Requirement: Any matter to be decided by the House must be brought in front of the House in the form of a motion. A motion can be moved only with the approval of the presiding officer.
  • 2. Motion Definition: The motion refers to a proposal that is placed before the meeting, for the purpose of discussion and decision.
  • 3. No Recording: Not recorded in the minutes of the meeting.
  • 4. No Binding: There is no binding.
  • 5. Amendment Provision: Amendments can be made, prior to voting on the motion.
  • 6. Withdrawal Right: The motion may be withdrawn by the member who proposes it before the motion is put to vote.
  • 7. Three Kinds: There are 3 kinds of motions — Substantive, Substitute and Subsidiary Motions.
RESOLUTIONS
  • 1. House Opinion: Resolutions are used to express opinion of the house. It is a self-contained independent proposal submitted for the approval of the House and drafted in such a way as to be capable of expressing a decision of the House.
  • 2. Passed Motion: Once the motion is passed it turns out as a resolution, i.e. it shows the general opinion of all members.
  • 3. Minutes Recording: It is recorded in the minutes of the meeting.
Page 14
  • 1. Binding Nature: There is binding.
  • 2. Amendment Resolution: As and when the resolution is passed, it can be amended by passing an amendment resolution.
  • 3. No Withdrawal: Once the resolution is approved, withdrawal is not possible.
POLICY CUT

1. Policy Cut Motion: That the amount of the demand be reduced to Re.1/- representing disapproval of the policy underlying the demand. A member giving notice of such a motion shall indicate in precise terms the particulars of the policy which he proposes to discuss. The discussion should be confined to the specific point or points mentioned in the notice and it shall be open to members to advocate an alternative policy.

ECONOMY CUT

1. Economy Cut Motion: The objective of the motion is to reduce the amount of the expenditure and the form of the motion is "The amount of the demand be reduced by Rupee... (a specified amount)". Such specified amount may be either a lump sum reduction in the demand or omission or reduction of an item in the demand.

TOKEN CUT

1. Token Cut Motion: The objective of the motion is to ventilate a specific grievance within the sphere of responsibility of the Government of India and its form is "The amount of the demand be reduced by Rupee 100".

CLOSURE MOTION
  • 1. Closure Purpose: It is a motion moved by a member to cut short the debate on a matter before the House.
  • 2. Debate Stopped: If the motion is approved by the House, debate is stopped forthwith and the matter is put to vote.
  • 3. Simple Closure: Simple Closure is one when a member moves that the matter having been sufficiently discussed be now put to vote.
  • 4. Closure by Compartments: In Closure by Compartments the clauses of a bill or a lengthy resolution are grouped into parts before the commencement of the debate.
  • 5. Part Voting: The debate covers the part as a whole and the entire part is put to vote.
  • 6. Kangaroo Closure: Under Kangaroo Closure only important clauses are taken up for debate and voting and the intervening clauses are skipped over and taken as passed.
  • 7. Guillotine Closure: Guillotine Closure is one when the undiscussed clauses of a bill or a resolution are also put to vote along with the discussed ones due to want of time.
HALF-AN-HOUR DISCUSSION {RULE - 55}
  • 1. Discussion Purpose: It is meant for discussing a matter of sufficient public importance, which has been subjected to a lot of debate and the answer to which needs elucidation on a matter of fact.
  • 2. Speaker Allotment: The Speaker can allot three days in a week for such discussions.
Page 15
  • 1. No Formal Motion: There is no formal motion or voting before the House.
POINT OF ORDER
  • 1. Point of Order Scope: A point of order should relate to the interpretation or enforcement of the Rules of the House or such articles of the Constitution that regulate the business of the House and should raise a question that is within the cognizance of the Speaker.
  • 2. Opposition Control: It is usually raised by an opposition member in order to control the government.
  • 3. Extraordinary Device: It is an extraordinary device as it suspends the proceedings before the House.
  • 4. No Debate Allowed: No debate is allowed on a point of order.
SPECIAL MENTION
  • 1. Special Mention Scope: A matter which is not a point of order or which cannot be raised during question hour, half-an-hour discussion, short duration discussion or under adjournment motion, calling attention notice or under any rule of the House can be raised under the special mention in the Rajya Sabha.
  • 2. Lok Sabha Equivalent: Its equivalent procedural device in the Lok Sabha is known as 'Notice (Mention) Under Rule 377'.
NO-DAY-YET-NAMED-MOTION
  • 1. Motion Admitted: It is a motion that has been admitted by the Speaker but no date has been fixed for its discussion.
  • 2. Day Allotment: The Speaker, after considering the state of business in the House and in consultation with the leader of the House or on the recommendation of the Business Advisory Committee, allots a day or days or part of a day for the discussion of such a motion.
LAME DUCK SESSION
  • 1. Last Session: It refers to the last session of the existing Lok Sabha, after a new Lok Sabha has been elected.
  • 2. Lame Ducks: Those members of the existing Lok Sabha who could not get re-elected to the new Lok Sabha are called lame-ducks.
SHADOW CABINET
  • 1. UK Practice: A Parliament practice prevalent in the UK where senior members of the Opposition cover the areas of responsibility of the actual cabinet.
  • 2. Future Cabinet: They will form the cabinet if their party is elected to the government.

WHIP
  • 1. Party Whip: Every political party, whether ruling or opposition has its own whip in the Parliament.

  • 3. Behaviour Regulation: He regulates and monitors their behaviour in the Parliament and the members are supposed to follow the directives given by the whip.

RULE 184

RULE 193

BILLS IN PARLIAMENT
ORDINARY BILLS
  • 1. Subject Matter: Anything under the Union and Concurrent Lists.
  • 2. Introduction: Introduced in either House.
  • 3. Majority Required: Simple majority in each House.
MONEY BILLS
  • 1. Subject Matter: Involving taxation, borrowing, govt funding, payment or withdrawal of money from the Consolidated or Contingency Funds of India.
  • 2. Introduction: Introduced only in LS.
  • 3. Majority Required: Simple majority in LS.
  • 4. RS Limitation: RS can recommend changes but LS has the right to reject them.
  • 5. Deemed Passed: RS must return or pass a Bill within 14 days or it is deemed passed.
DIFFERENCES BETWEEN ORDINARY AND MONEY BILL
ORDINARY BILL
  • 1. Introduction House: Can be introduced in either Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha.
  • 2. Introducer: Can be introduced by either minister or member.
  • 3. President Recommendation: Can be introduced without President's recommendation.
  • 4. RS Power: Can be amended/rejected by Rajya Sabha.
  • 5. Detention Period: Rajya Sabha can only detain bill for 6 months.
  • 6. No Certification: Does not require certification of speaker when transmitted to Rajya Sabha.
  • 7. Joint Sitting: Sent for President's assent if passed by both houses. In case of deadlock, joint sitting can be summoned.
  • 8. Government Defeat: Its defeat in Lok Sabha may lead to resignation of the government.
  • 9. President Action: Can be rejected, approved or returned for reconsideration by the President.
MONEY BILL
  • 1. Introduction House: Can only be introduced in Lok Sabha.
  • 2. Introducer: Can be introduced only by the minister.
  • 3. President Recommendation: Can be introduced only with President's recommendation.
  • 4. RS Power: Rajya Sabha can only return the bill.
  • 5. Detention Period: Rajya Sabha can detain bills for max 14 days.
  • 6. Speaker Certification: Requires Speaker's certification.
Page 18
  • 1. President's Assent: Can be sent for president's assent even if approved only by Lok Sabha. No provision for joint sitting.
  • 2. Government Defeat: Defeat in Lok Sabha leads to resignation of the government.
  • 3. President Action: Can be rejected or approved but cannot be returned by the President.

  • 1. Money Bill Origin: (1) A Money Bill shall not be introduced in the Council of States.

ARTICLE 110
  • 1. Money Bill Definition: (1) For the purposes of this Chapter, a Bill shall be deemed to be a Money Bill if it contains only provisions dealing with all or any of the following matters, namely:—

    • b. Borrowing Regulation: (b) the regulation of the borrowing of money or the giving of any guarantee by the Government of India, or the amendment of the law with respect to any financial obligations undertaken or to be undertaken by the Government of India;
    • c. Consolidated Fund: (c) the custody of the Consolidated Fund or the Contingency Fund of India, the payment of moneys into or the withdrawal of moneys from any such fund;

    • f. Receipt and Audit: (f) the receipt of money on account of the Consolidated Fund of India or the public account of India or the custody or issue of such money or the audit of the accounts of the Union or of a State; or
    • g. Incidental Matters: (g) any matter incidental to any of the matters specified in sub-clauses (a) to (f).
  • 2. Exclusion Clause: (2) A Bill shall not be deemed to be a Money Bill by reason only that it provides for the imposition of fines or other pecuniary penalties, or for the demand or payment of fees for licences or fees for services rendered, or by reason that it provides for the imposition, abolition, remission, alteration or regulation of any tax by any local authority or body for local purposes.

COMMITTEE ON ESTIMATES
  • 1. Lok Sabha Committee: The Estimates Committee is a Committee of Lok Sabha.
  • 2. First Constitution: The motion for election of the first Estimates Committee was adopted by the Provisional Parliament on 3rd April, 1950 and the Committee was elected on 10th April 1950.
  • 3. Member Strength: The Committee consist of not more than 30 Members from Lok Sabha who shall be elected by the House every year from amongst its members according principle of promotional representation by means of single transferable vote.
  • 4. Chairman Selection: The Speaker is empowered to appoint the Chairman of the Committee from amongst its members.
  • 5. Oversight Role: It assists Parliament in overseeing pre-budget estimates of the government, and examines whether the proposed expenditure is within the limits of government policy.
  • 6. Reform Reporting: It also reports on administrative reforms needed within Ministries and suggests policies to bring about efficiency in administration.
  • 7. Pre-1993 Role: Prior to 1993, this Committee carried out the task of examining proposed estimates of expenditure by various ministries.
COMMITTEE ON PUBLIC UNDERTAKINGS
  • 1. Committee Origin: The Krishna Menon Committee recommended that this committee be formed in 1964.
  • 2. Member Composition: This financial committee is made up of 22 people - 15 members are elected from the Lok Sabha and 7 members are elected from the Rajya Sabha.
  • 3. Election Method: The members of the committee are chosen annually by the Parliament from among its members, using the Proportional Representation and the Single Transferable Vote system.
  • 4. Chairperson Appointment: The chairperson of the committee is appointed by the Speaker of the Lok Sabha.
  • 5. Chairman Source: The Chairman of the Committee on Public Enterprises is chosen from among the Lok Sabha members.

DEPARTMENT STANDING COMMITTEES
  • 1. Committee Allocation: There are a total of 24 Departmental Standing Committees:- 8 under Rajya Sabha and 16 under Lok Sabha.
  • 2. Administration Exclusion: They don't consider day-to-day administration.
  • 3. Non-Interference Rule: They generally don't intervene into matters taken up by another departmental standing committees.
  • 4. Advisory Nature: Recommendations made them are advisory in nature, hence are not binding on the Parliament.
  • 5. Demand for Grants: They work upon the demand for grants of the concerned ministries. They don't propose any cut-motion.

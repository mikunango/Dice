POLITY

CH6- Citizenship

Part II - Article 5 to 11

  • 1. Domicile-Based Citizenship: Every person who has domicile in the territory of India and (ARTICLE 5) Who was born in the territory of India; or either of whose parents was born in the territory of India; or who has been ordinarily resident in the territory of India for not less than five years immediately preceding such commencement; shall be a citizen of India.
  • 2. Migration from Pakistan (Pre-July 1948): Persons who migrated to India from Pakistan before 19 July, 1948 and since then have been ordinarily residing in India. (ARTICLE 6)
  • 3. Migration from Pakistan (Post-July 1948): Persons who migrate to India from Pakistan on or after 19 July, 1948 but got themselves, duly registered as citizen with a competent officer.
  • 4. Return from Pakistan: Persons who migrated to Pakistan after 1st March, 1947 but returned to India under a permit for resettlement. (ARTICLE 7)
  • 5. Indian Birth Lineage: Any person who or either of whose parents or any of whose grandparents was born in India.
  • 6. Indian Origin Abroad: Rights of citizenship of certain persons of Indian origin residing outside India. (ARTICLE 8)
  • 7. Parliamentary Regulation: Parliament to regulate the right of citizenship by law. (ARTICLE 11)
ACQUISITION OF INDIAN CITIZENSHIP — THE CITIZENSHIP ACT 1955
BY BIRTH
  • 1. Birth-Based Citizenship: Any person born in India, on or after 26th January 1950 but prior to the commencement of the 1986 Act on 1st July 1987, is a citizen of India if either of his parent was a citizen of India at the time of the birth.
    • a. Post-2004 Birth Condition: Further, those born in India on or after 3rd December 2004 are considered citizens of India only if both their parents are citizens of India or if one parent is a citizen of India and the other not an illegal migrant at the time of their birth.
BY DESCENT
  • 2. Pre-1992 Descent Rule: Person born outside India on or after 26th January 1950 but before 10th December 1992 are citizen of India by descent if their father was a citizen of India at the time of their birth.
  • 3. Post-1992 Descent Rule: Persons born outside India on or after 10th December 1992 are considered as citizen of India if either of their parents is a citizen of India at the time of their birth.
    • a. Consulate Registration: From 3rd December 2004 onwards, person born outside India shall not be considered citizens of India unless their birth is registered at an Indian consulate within one year with the permission of the central government.
BY REGISTRATION
  • 4. Registration Eligibility: Any person, who is not a citizen by virtue of the Constitution or the provisions of the Citizenship Act and belongs to any of the following categories, can apply for registration as a citizen.

BY NATURALIZATION
  • 1. Naturalization Conditions: A foreigner, on application for naturalization to a competent authority appointed by the State, can acquire Indian citizenship provided he satisfies certain conditions like having normal resided for at least twelve years in India in a period of 14 years before making an application.
BY INCORPORATION OF TERRITORY
  • 1. Territory Incorporation: If any new territory becomes a part of India, the Government of India, the Government of India shall notify the persons of that territory to be citizens of India.
TERMINATION OF INDIAN CITIZENSHIP
RENUNCIATION
  • 1. Voluntary Renunciation: If any citizen of India who is also a national of another country renounces his Indian citizenship through a declaration in the prescribed manner, he ceases to be an Indian citizen. (ARTICLE 9)
  • 2. Minor Child Impact: When a male person ceases to be a citizen of India, every minor child of his also ceases to be a citizen of India. However, such a child may within one year after attaining full age become an Indian citizen by making a declaration of his intention to resume Indian citizen-ship.
TERMINATION
  • 1. Voluntary Adoption: Indian citizenship can be terminated if a citizen knowingly or voluntarily adopts the citizenship of any foreign country.
DEPRIVATION
  • 1. Government Deprivation Power: The government of India can deprive a person of his citizenship in some cases. But this is not applicable for all citizens.
  • 2. Limited Applicability: It is applicable only in the case of citizens who have acquired the citizenship by registration, naturalisation or only by Article 5 Clause (c)

PERSON OF INDIAN ORIGIN (PIO) CARDS & OVERSEAS CITIZENSHIP OF INDIA (OCI) CARDS
  • 1. NRI Definition: A NON-RESIDENT INDIAN (NRI) is a citizen of India who has temporarily emigrated to another country for six months.
  • 2. PIO & OCI Status: PIOs and OCI card holders are not citizens but people who want to stay connected and involved with India more closely.
    • a. PIO Card Origin: The PIO card was first implemented in 2002 as a benefit to foreign nationals who could establish at least a third generation tie to Indian origin.
    • b. OCI Card Origin: The OCI card was implemented in 2005, carried more benefits than the PIO card, and is valid for the holder's lifetime.

PIO-OCI Merger: In 2015, the PIO scheme was withdrawn and was merged with the OCI.

BENEFITS OF PIO
  • 1. Visa Exemption: A PIO card holder doesn't need a visa to visit India.
  • 2. Employment & Academic Access: The holder also doesn't require a student or employment visa to acquire employment or academic opportunities in India.
  • 3. FRRO Exemption: The holder was exempted from registering at the foreigner regional registration office (FRRO) during the duration of stay in India.
  • 4. NRI Parity: The holder also enjoys parity with NRIs in concern to economic, financial and educational matters like property transfer or acquisition, holding, disposal, investment, admission of children in educational institutions under general category quota for NRIs.
  • 5. Immigration Facilitation: Separate immigration counters are provided at all International airports.
ADDITIONAL BENEFITS OF OCI
  • 1. Citizenship Pathway: Apart from the benefits of PIO, the OCI can attain Indian citizenship and then live in India for a period of one year including short breaks, if they remain an OCI for 5 years.
  • 2. Banking & Investment: An OCI cards holder can open special bank accounts in India just like NRIs and make investments.
  • 3. Property Rights: They can also buy non-farm property and exercise ownership rights.
  • 4. Document Access: They apply for a driver's license and PAN card.
  • 5. NRI-Equivalent Benefits: They get same economic, financial and educational benefits like NRIs and they can also adopt children.

Restrictions on PIO & OCI: Both OCI and PIO cannot vote, hold a government job or purchase agricultural or farm land. They also cannot run for public office or travel to restricted areas without permission.

Usual Resident Definition

According to the Home Ministry, a "usual resident of the country" is one who has been residing in a local area for at least the last six months or intends to stay in a particular location for the next six months.

  • 7. NPR Scope: NPR is not a citizenship enumeration drive, as it would record even a foreign national staying in a locality for more than six months.
    • a. NPR vs NRC Distinction: This makes NPR different from the NRC, which includes only Indian citizens while seeking to identify and exclude non-citizens.
  • 8. Legal Framework: The NPR is being prepared under provisions of the Citizenship Act, 1955 and the Citizenship (Registration of Citizens and issue of National Identity Cards) Rules, 2003.
  • 9. Mandatory Registration: It is mandatory for every "usual resident of India" to register in the NPR.
    • a. Assam Exclusion: Only Assam will not be included (as per a notification by the Registrar General of India in August), given the recently completed NRC in that state.
  • 10. Census Conjunction: NPR will be conducted in conjunction with the house-listing phase, the first phase of the Census, by the Office of Registrar General of India (RGI) for Census 2021.
  • 11. Multi-Level Conduct: It is conducted at the local, sub-district, district, state and national levels.
  • 12. RGI Pilot Project: The RGI has already begun a pilot project in 1,200 villages and 40 towns and cities through 5,218 enumeration blocks where it is collecting various data from people.

13. NPR Historical Data: The data for NPR was first collected in 2010 along with the house-listing phase of Census 2011. In 2015, this data was updated by conducting door-to-door surveys.

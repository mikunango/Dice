CH7 - Fundamental Rights

Part III — Article 12 to 35

1. Rights Recognition: Some of rights that are recognized by the state and enshrined in the constitution are called fundamental rights.

2. Basic Human Freedoms: The Fundamental Rights are defined as the basic human freedoms which every individual has a right to enjoy for a proper and harmonious development of personality.

3. Bombay Session Demand: The Indian National Congress at its Bombay session in August 1918 demanded the inclusion of declaration of rights of the people of India as the British citizens in the new Government of India Act. The Declaration Included amongst other things guarantees in regard to equality before the law, protection in respect to liberty, life and property, freedom of speech and press and right of association.

4. Commonwealth Bill 1925: The Common Wealth of India Bill finalized by the National Convention of 1925 embodied a specific declaration of rights.

5. Madras Session 1927: The resolution passed by Indian National Congress in 1927 at its Madras Session lay down that the declaration of Fundamental Rights should be the basis of future Constitution of India.

6. Nehru Committee: The Nehru Committee observed that the first care should be to have Fundamental Rights guaranteed in such a manner which will not permit its withdrawal under any circumstances.

7. Karachi Session 1931: The Indian National Congress at its Karachi session in 1931 again demanded for a written guarantee for Fundamental Rights in any future Constitutional setup in India.

8. Trade Union Memorandum: The memorandum of the National trade Union Federation submitted to the joint committee on Indian constitution reforms (1932–33), the memorandum submitted by M. Venkatarangaiah to the Sapru committee and the Sapru committee proposals provided for various fundamental rights that the people of free India should get.

9. Advisory Sub-Committees: The advisory committee in turn constituted on Feb27, 1947 five sub-committees which would deal with fundamental rights.

Objectives

1. Dignity & Personality: The Supreme court held in Menaka Gandhi v/s Union of India that these fundamental rights represent and basic value cherished by the people of this country since the Vedic times and they are calculated to protect the dignity of the individual and create conditions in which every human being can develop his personality to the fullest extent.

2. Inviolable Rights: The aim behind having a declaration of fundamental rights is to make inviolable certain elementary rights appertaining to the individual and to keep them unaffected by the shifting majorities in the legislatures.

3. Against State Interference: It is to preserve certain basic human rights against interference the state.

4. Democratic Polity: These rights are basic to a democratic polity.

5. New National Existence: It not only ensure the inviolability of certain essential rights against political vicissitudes but also to impress upon the people the fact of their having reached a new level of national existence.

6. Free Society Requirement: The guarantee of certain basic human rights is an indispensable requirement of a free society.

Features
  • 1. Sacrosanct Nature: These rights have been declared as sacrosanct, inalienable and inviolable.
  • 2. State Obligation: It is mandatory for state to provide fundamental rights.
  • 3. Reasonable Restrictions: The state can impose reasonable restrictions on these rights, thus making them qualified and not absolute.
  • 4. SC Guarantee: These fundamental rights are defended and guaranteed by the Supreme Court of India.
  • 5. Emergency Suspension: These rights can be suspended during the operation of a National Emergency except the rights defined under Article 20 and 21.
  • 6. Parliamentary Restriction: The application of these rights can be restricted or abrogated by the parliament (Article 33), rights of armed forces, police forces, intelligence agencies, etc. can be restricted by this Article.
  • 7. Amendability: Fundamental Rights may be amended with out affecting the basic features (Keshawanand Bharti case).
  • 8. Secular Fabric: Fundamental Rights strengthen the secular fabric of the Indian State.
  • 9. Minority Protection: Fundamental Rights protect the interest of minorities.
  • 10. Judicial Criteria: Fundamental Rights give Judiciary clear criteria as to how the regulation of relations between the citizens and the government will take place.
  • 11. Child Education Right: Fundamental Rights empower the young children of our nation as they are granted the right to receive free education up to the age of 14. (Article 21A)
  • 12. Individual Liberty: Fundamental Rights form a defensive wall of individual liberty.
Criticism

1. Kapoor's Critique: The chapter dealing with the fundamental rights should be renamed as 'Limitations on Fundamental Rights' or 'Fundamental Rights and Limitations Thereon'. Jaspat Roy Kapoor

  • 2. Excessive Restrictions: FR are subjected to innumerable exceptions, restrictions, qualifications, and explanations.
  • 3. No Socio-Economic Rights: It makes no provision for important social and economic rights like the right to social security, right to work, right to employment and so on.
  • 4. Vague Terminology: The various phrases and words used in the chapter like 'public order, 'minorities', 'reasonable restriction', 'public interest and so on are not clearly defined.
  • 5. Complicated Language: The language is very complicated and beyond the comprehension of the common man. The Constitution of India a 'paradise for lawyers'(Jennings).
  • 6. Not Immutable: They are not sacrosanct or immutable as the Parliament can curtail or abolish them.
  • 7. Emergency Suspension: The suspension of their enforcement during the operation of National Emergency is another blot on the efficacy of these rights.
  • 8. Judicial Process Barrier: The judicial process hinders the common man from getting his rights enforced through the courts.
  • 9. Preventive Detention: The provision for preventive detention (Article 22) takes away the spirit and substance of the fundamental rights.
  • 10. No Consistent Philosophy: They are based on no consistent philosophy(Jennings). This creates difficulty for the Court in interpreting the rights.
  • 11. Political Opponents Shield: It is undeniable that they have generally proven useful in protecting the rights of the political opponents of the government against the might of the State.

12. Democratic Bedrock: In spite of the above criticism and shortcomings, the Fundamental Rights are significant as they constitute the bedrock of democratic system and serve as a formidable bulwark of individual liberty.

Fundamental Rights Are Not Absolute

1. Qualified Rights: Our constitution grants some fundamental rights with one hand and takeaway with the other. Fundamental rights are essential for the existence and moral development of the people. Yet reasonable restrictions can be imposed in the larger interest of the society.

2. Reasonable Classification: While article 14 prohibits class legislation yet doctrine of Reasonable Classification is permitted to treat differently placed people differently (State of Bombay v Balsara).

  • a. Intelligible Differentia: The classification must be made on an intelligible differentiating factor and have a reasonable nexus with the object. (Das J. in State of W.B. v Anwar Ali Sarkar).

3. Article 19 Restrictions: Article 19 grants the several freedom such as right to speech and expression, but it can be restricted by law under respective conditions mentioned in the clause 2.

  • a. Public Interest Test: The reasonability of the restriction should be judged from the aspect of the general public's interest (Mohd. Hanif Quershi v State of Bihar)

4. Right to Life Limitation: Right to Life (Article 21)is limited by the procedure established by law.

5. Religious Freedom Limits: Article 25 provides for equal right to profess, practice, and propagate any religion.

  • a. Hatred Exclusion: Creation of hatred among groups while practising religion, which can have possible ramifications over public order as well as health, was held to be outside the scope of freedom of religion (Subhash Desai v Sharad J. Rao)

Doctrine of Severability

1. Severability Principle: The doctrine of Severability says that is if a part of any law is inconsistent then the rest of the part will remain valid. It applies to both pre and post-constitutional law.

  • 2. Doctrine of Separability: It is also known as doctrine of separability.
  • 3. Extent of Inconsistency: According to the clauses of Article 13, a law is void only "to the extent of the inconsistency or contravention"with the relevant Fundamental Right.
  • 4. Partial Voidness: It means that an Act may not be void as a whole; only a part of it may be void and if that part is severable from the rest which is valid, then the rest may continue to stand and remain operative.
  • 5. Inseparable Invalidity: If both the valid and invalid part are so closely mix up with each other that it cannot be separated then the whole law or act will be held invalid.
  • 6. Gopalan Case: The Supreme court held in Gopalan v. State of Madras case that
    • a. Repugnant Provision Void: ❖ In case of repugnancyto the Constitution, only the repugnant provision of the impugned (रद्द) Act will be void and not the whole of it, and every attempt should be made to save as much as possible of the act.
  • 7. RMDC Judgment: In R.M.D.C. v. Union of India judgment completely observed Doctrine of severability.
    • a. Inextricable Mixing: ❖ If the valid and invalid provisions are so inextricably mixed up that they cannot be separated from one another, then the invalidity of a portion must result in the invalidity of the Act in its entirety.
    • b. Distinct & Separate: ❖ On the other hand, if they are so distinct and separate that after striking out what is invalid, what remains is in itself a complete code independent of the rest, then it will be upheld notwithstanding that the rest has become unenforceable.

Article 14

1. Equality Provision: The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India.

  • a. Democratic Equality: Democracy can only thrive and flourish where the individuals in the society are treated equally and without discrimination.
  • b. No Legal Discrimination: The Right to equality means the absence of legal discrimination only on grounds of caste, race, religion, sex, and place of birth and ensures equal rights to all citizens.
  • c. Positive & Negative: It is both a positive equality as well as a negative right.
    • i. ❖ Negative Right > Prohibits unequal treatment
    • ii. ❖ Positive Right > Demands to be treated equally

2. Unreasonable Discrimination: Article 14 lays down the general principles of equality before the law or the equal protection of laws and prohibits unreasonable discrimination between the persons.

  • a. Positive Concept: Equal protection of laws is a more positive concept implying equality of treatment in the equal circumstances.

3. Rule of Law Corollary: The Doctrine of Equality before law is a necessary corollary of Rule of Law which pervades the Indian constitution. The underlying object of Article 14 is to secure all persons, citizens or non-citizens, the equality of status and opportunity referrers Preamble to our constitution.

4. Dynamic Concept: The Supreme Court held in Sri Srinivasa Theatre v. Govt. of Tamil Nadu case that the equality before the law is a dynamic concept having many facets.

  • a. No Privileged Class: There shall be no privileged person or class and that none shall be above law.
  • b. Equal Society Mandate: The obligation upon the State to bring about, through the machinery of law, an equal society or, equality before the law can be predicated meaningfully only in equal society.

2. Exclusive Parliament Power: Parliament shall have, and the Legislature of a State shall not have, power to make laws—

3. Specified Matters: with respect to any of the matters which under clause (3) of article 16, clause (3) of article 32, article 33 and article 34 may be provided for by law made by Parliament; and

4. Offence Punishment: (ii) for prescribing punishment for those acts which are declared to be offences under this Part; and Parliament shall, as soon as may be after the commencement of this Constitution, make laws for prescribing punishment for the acts referred to in sub-clause (ii);

5. Existing Law Continuance: any law in force immediately before the commencement of this Constitution in the territory of India with respect to any of the matters referred to in sub-clause (i) of clause (a) or providing for punishment for any act referred to in sub-clause (ii) of that clause shall, subject to the terms thereof and to any adaptations and modifications that may be made therein under article 372, continue in force until altered or repealed or amended by Parliament.

INDIAN POLITY

CH10- Vice-President

Part V - Article 63 to 69

Article 63

There shall be a Vice-President of India.

Article 64

The Vice-President to be ex officio Chairman of the Council of States.

Article 65

The Vice-President to act as President or to discharge his functions during casual vacancies in the office, or during the absence, of President.

Article 66
  • 1. Electoral College: The Vice-President shall be elected by the members of an electoral college consisting of the members of both Houses of Parliament in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote and the voting at such election shall be by secret ballot.
  • 2. Membership Restriction: The Vice-President shall not be a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State, and if a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State be elected Vice-President, he shall be deemed to have vacated his seat in that House on the date on which he enters upon his office as Vice-President.
  • 3. Eligibility Conditions: No person shall be eligible for election as Vice-President unless he —
    • a. Citizenship: is a citizen of India;
    • b. Age Requirement: has completed the age of thirty-five years; and
    • c. Council Qualification: is qualified for election as a member of the Council of States.
  • 4. Office of Profit Bar: A person shall not be eligible for election as Vice-President if he holds any office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State or under any local or other authority subject to the control of any of the said Governments.
Page 110
Article 67
  • 1. Term of Office: The Vice-President shall hold office for a term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office:

Provided that —

  • a. Resignation: a Vice-President may, by writing under his hand addressed to the President, resign his office;
  • b. Removal Process: a Vice-President may be removed from his office by a resolution of the Council of States passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council and agreed to by the House of the People; but no resolution for the purpose of this clause shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution;
  • c. Continuation Clause: a Vice-President shall, notwithstanding the expiration of his term, continue to hold office until his successor enters upon his office.
Article 68
  • 1. Term Expiry Vacancy: An election to fill a vacancy caused by the expiration of the term of office of Vice-President shall be completed before the expiration of the term.
  • 2. Casual Vacancy: An election to fill a vacancy in the office of Vice-President occurring by reason of his death, resignation or removal, or otherwise shall be held as soon as possible after the occurrence of the vacancy, and the person elected to fill the vacancy shall, subject to the provisions of article 67, be entitled to hold office for the full term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office.
Article 69

Every Vice-President shall, before entering upon his office, make and subscribe before the President, or some person appointed in that behalf by him.

CH10(B)- Prime Minister

Prime Minister is the real custodian of all executive authority

Article 75
  • 1. Presidential Appointment: The Prime Minister shall be appointed by the President
  • 2. Majority Leader: President appoints the leader of the majority in the Lok Sabha as the Prime Minister.
  • 3. Presidential Discretion: In case no party gets a majority and some parties are even unable to elect a common candidate as their leader, the President can play a real role in the appointment of the Prime Minister.
    • a. House Eligibility: The Prime Minister can be from either House of the Parliament.
    • b. Essential Condition: The only essential condition is that he must be the adopted or elected leader of majority in the Lok Sabha.
    • c. Non-Member Provision: Any person who is not a member of either House of the Parliament can also becomes a minister or the Prime Minister and e he can remain so for six months, within this period he has to essentially get the membership of either House.
    • d. Pleasure Doctrine: The Prime Minister holds office during the pleasure of the President.
  • 4. No-Confidence Motion: Lok Sabha can pass a vote of no-confidence against him and in this case the Prime Minister either submits his resignation to the President.
Powers and Functions
Article 74
  • 1. Council of Ministers: There shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President.
Article 75
  • 1. Ministerial Appointment: the other Ministers shall be appointed by the President on the advice of the Prime Minister.
  • 1A. 15% Ceiling: The total number of Ministers, including the Prime Minister, in the Council of Ministers shall not exceed fifteen per cent. of the total number of members of the House of the People. (Ninety-first Amendment) Act, 2003),
  • 2. Pleasure of President: The Ministers shall hold office during the pleasure of the President.
  • 3. Collective Responsibility: The Council of Ministers shall be collectively responsible to the House of the People.
  • 5. Six-Month Rule: A Minister who for any period of six consecutive months is not a member of either House of Parliament shall at the expiration of that period cease to be a Minister.

Article 78

It shall be the duty of the Prime Minister—

  • a. Communication Duty: to communicate to the President all decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to the administration of the affairs of the Union and proposals for legislation;
  • b. Information Duty: to furnish such information relating to the administration of the affairs of the Union and proposals for legislation as the President may call for ; and
  • c. Submission Duty: if the President so requires, to submit for the consideration of the Council of Ministers any matter on which a decision has been taken by a Minister but which has not been considered by the Council.
    • d. Portfolio Allocation: Allocation of Portfolios
    • e. Presidential Link: Link between the President and the Cabinet
    • f. Parliamentary Leader: Leader of the Parliament
    • g. Dissolution Power: Power to get the Parliament Dissolved
Position of the Prime Minister
  • 1. Executive Duality: The President is the de jure executive and Prime Minister is the de facto executive(real) in the scheme of the parliamentary system of government provided by the constitution. The general accepted theory is that the Prime Minister is just like primus inter pares (first among equals).
  • 1. Concentration of Power: This Constitution concentrates so much power and influence in the hands of the Prime Minister that there is every danger to apprehend that the Prime Minister may become a dictator if he chooses to do so . (K.T. Shah)
  • 2. Council Dependency: Since the Prime Minister stands at the head of the council of ministers, the other ministers cannot function when the Prime Minister resigns or dies .
  • 2. Vacancy Effect: The resignation or death of an incumbent Prime Minister automatically dissolves the council of ministers and thereby generates a vacuum.
  • 4. Dissolution Advisory: The Prime Minister has the power to advise the President in favour of a dissolution of the Lok Sabha.
  • 3. Member Dependency: Means that the members hold their seats in the House at the mercy of the Prime Minister.
  • 6. Lower House Leader: The Prime Minister is the leader of the Lower House. In this capacity, he enjoys the following powers -
  • 4. Session Advisory: He advises the President about summoning and proroguing of the sessions of the Parliament.
  • 5. Dissolution Recommendation: He can recommend the dissolution of the Lok Sabha to President at any time.
  • 6. Policy Announcements: He announces government policies on the floor of the House.
  • 10. Chief Presidential Advisor: The constitution assigns to the Prime Minister the role of being the chief advisor to the President. All the powers of the President, both the normal powers and the emergency powers, are really the powers of the Prime Minister.
  • 7. Binding Advice: The President is now bound to act according to the advice of the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister. {Article 74(1)}

Appointment and Composition of Ministers
  • 1. Appointment Authority: Appointments of the Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers are done by the President of India.
  • 2. PM's Recommendation: In the case of the appointment of the Council of Ministers, the Prime Minister's recommendation holds greater value.
    • a. Parliamentary Membership: They are members of either of the Houses of the Parliament;
    • b. Six-Month Membership: If they are not members of the Parliament Houses, then within a span of six months he/she must become a member by means of nomination, or election.
  • 3. Cabinet Ministers: The Cabinet Ministers
  • 4. Ministers of State: The Ministers of State – Independent and attached to the Cabinet Ministers
  • 5. Deputy Ministers: Deputy Ministers
    • a. Meeting Restriction: These ministers are restricted from attending Cabinet meetings unless specially invited.
  • 6. Parliamentary Secretaries: The parliamentary secretaries are considered to be another group of ministers who are attached to the senior group of ministers and function as an assistant to them to discharge parliamentary duties.
Oaths of Office
  • 1. Constitutional Allegiance: To bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India,
  • 2. Sovereignty & Integrity: To uphold the sovereignty and integrity of India,
  • 3. Faithful Discharge: To faithfully and conscientiously discharge the duties of his office, and
  • 4. Impartial Justice: To do right to all manner of people in accordance with the Constitution, and the law, without fear or favor, affection or ill will.

  • 1. Sumptuary Allowance: The salary of a minister is the same as a member of the Parliament but the sumptuary allowance is differ for the Prime Minister, the Cabinet Minister, for a Minister of State and for a Deputy Minister.
Responsibility
  • 1. Collective Responsibility: Collective Responsibility refers to ministers owning joint responsibility for their actions to the House of People, the Lok Sabha.
    • a. Cabinet Binding: The Cabinet Ministers are bound by the Cabinet's decision irrespective of whether the former accept it or not .
  • 2. Individual Responsibility: Individual Responsibility means the ministers holding office on the wish of the President, they can be removed by the President whenever he feels the need .
  • 3. No Legal Responsibility: The ministers are not vested with any kind of Legal Responsibility.
    • a. Judicial Review Bar: The courts are also barred from reviewing the advice given by the Council of Ministers to the President.
Differences B/W the Council of Ministers and Cabinet
  • 1. Wider Body: The Council of Ministers is a much wider body in comparison to the Cabinet.
  • 2. Cabinet Subset: The Cabinet is a part of the Council of Ministers.
  • 3. Collective Functions: The Cabinet is vested with collective functions.
  • 4. Decision-Implementation Split: The Cabinet makes decisions, the Council of Ministers is responsible for implementing the same.
  • 5. Accountability Enforcement: The Council of Ministers is collectively responsible towards the Lok Sabha, the Cabinet is responsible for enforcing such responsibility on the Council.
Collective Responsibility is a Myth in Indian Cabinet System.
  • 1. Constitutional Convention: Collective responsibility is a constitutional convention in Parliamentary systems which means members of the cabinet must publicly support all governmental decisions made in Cabinet, even if they do not privately agree with them . Article 75(3) states that the Council of Ministers shall be collectively responsible to the House of the People.
  • 1. Cabinet Solidarity: Solidarity within the cabinet can strengthen the Prime minister and accelerate policy decisions and interests of that party.
  • 2. Coalition Challenge: Since Independence, several Prime ministers had difficulty in enforcing collective responsibility and becomes a bigger challenge when there is a coalition government.
  • 2. Public Criticism: Till now several ministers publicly criticized the government decisions and violated the principle of collective responsibility.
  • 3. Cabinet Resignations: There have been a number of resignations in the past because of the differences with the Cabinet.
  • 4. Governance Dysfunction: The inability of the Government to function on the basis of collective responsibility will be an important cause of -
  • 4. Executive Contradictions: Contradictions and disagreements between cabinet members of the executive branch.
  • 5. Team Benefit Loss: Loss of practical benefit of being part of a team.
  • 6. Loyalty Erosion: Loss of party loyalty and personal loyalty to the Prime minister.

  • 7. Development Delays: Heavy cost and time-escalation in implementing development projects.
  • 1. Fundamental Principle: The principle of Collective Responsibility may be regarded as fundamental to the working of the Parliamentary Government.
Kitchen Cabinet
  • 2. Inner Cabinet: A smaller body of ministers called the 'inner Cabinet' or Kitchen Cabinet, has become the real centre of power. This informal body consists consists of the prime minister and two to four influential colleagues in whom he has faith and with whom he can discuss every problem.
  • 3. Advisory Composition: It advises the prime minister on important political and administrative issues and assists him in making crucial decisions. It is composed of not only cabinet ministers but also outsiders like friends and family members of the prime minister.
Deputy Prime Minister
  • 1. Non-Constitutional Office: The deputy prime minister is not technically a constitutional office, it seldom carries any specific powers.
  • 1. Presidential Appointment: The Deputy Prime Minister is appointed by the President.
  • 2. PM's Nomination: The Deputy Prime minister candidate is nominated by the Prime Minister of India. The deputy prime minister of India is a member of the Union council of ministers. This post was inaugurated by Vallabhbhai Patel on 15 August 1947.
  • 3. Last Deputy PM: The last Deputy prime minister was L. K. Advani.
  • 4. First Among Equals: The deputy prime minister is treated as the "first among equals" in the cabinet.

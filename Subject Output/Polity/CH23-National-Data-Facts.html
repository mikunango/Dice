National Symbols, Emblems & Identity

State Emblem
a heraldic instrument or symbolic object as a unique insignia of a nation, organization, or family.

1. Lion Capital Adaptation: The State Emblem is an adaptation of the Lion Capital of Asoka at Sarnath. In the original, there are four lions, mounted back to back, on a circular abacus, which itself rests on a bell-shaped lotus. The frieze of the abacus has sculptures in high relief of an elephant, a galloping horse, a bull and a lion separated by intervening Dharma Chakras.

2. Adoption as State Emblem: The profile of the Lion Capital showing three lions mounted on the abacus with a Dharma Chakra in the centre, a bull on the right and a galloping horse on the left, and outlines of Dharma Chakras on the extreme right and left was adopted as the State Emblem of India on January 26, 1950.

3. Lotus Omission: The bell-shaped lotus was omitted.

4. Satyameva Jayate Motto: The motto Satyameva Jayate, which means 'Truth Alone Triumphs', written in Devanagari script below the profile of the Lion Capital is part of the State Emblem of India

  • 1. Power & Courage: Four lions seated back to back which imply power, courage, confidence and pride.
  • 2. Elephant Symbolism: The elephant denotes Buddha's outset.
  • 3. Bull Symbolism: The bull symbolizes Zodiac sign of Buddha- Taurus.
  • 4. Horse Symbolism: The horse signifies Buddha's horse, which he rode at the time of departing from the citadel.
  • 5. Lion Significance: The lion indicates enlightenment.
  • 6. Satyameva Jayate Origin: Slogan 'Satyameva Jayate' is a quote from the Mundaka Upanishad.
  • 7. Emblem Designer: Dinanath Bhargava is believed to visit Alipore Zoo in Kolkata to see a lion before he depicts the same on paper.
National Anthem

1. Original Composition: The 'Jana Gana Mana' was originally composed in Bengali by India's first Nobel laureate Rabindranath Tagore on December 11, 1911.

2. Brahmo Hymn Source: The parent song, 'Bharoto Bhagyo Bidhata' is a Brahmo hymn which has five verses and only the first verse has been adopted as National Anthem.

3. Unity in Diversity: If put forwards succinctly, the National Anthem conveys the spirit of pluralism or in more popular term the concept of 'Unity in Diversity', which lies at the core of India's cultural heritage.

4. English Translation: The idea of translating the song from Bengali to English came to Tagore while he was visiting the Besant Theosophical College on the invitation of Irish poet James H. Cousins.

5. Translation Location: He did the English translation during his stay at Madanapalle, in Chittoor of Andhra Pradesh.

6. First Performance: The song 'Bharat Bhagya Bidhata' was first sung at the annual session of the Indian National Congress in Calcutta on December 27, 1911.

National Song

1. Composition Origin: Bankim Chandra Chatterjee's poem Vande Maataram was written in highly sanskritized Bengali in his novel Anandamath in 1882 at Chinsura.

  • 1. First Rendition: Rabindranath Tagore performed the poem for the first time before the Indian National Congress in 1896.
  • 2. National Song Selection: The Congress Working Committee selected the first two verses of the song as the National Song of India in October 1937.
  • 3. Modern Composition: The modern Vande Mataram composition is credited to V. D. Paluskar, the founder of the Gandharva Mahavidyalaya and Akhil Bharatiya Gandharva Mahavidyalaya Mandal.
  • 4. English Translation: The Vande Mataram was translated into English by Nares Chandra Sen-Gupta, in 1906.
  • 5. Benares Session: In 1905, Sarala Devi Chaudurani sang the national song in the Benares Congress Session.
  • 6. First Publication: It was first published in Bankim's magazine Banga darshan to fill up a blank page.
National Calendar

1. Saka Era Origin: The Saka Samvat, a historic Hindu calendar that was eventually established as the 'Indian National Calendar' in 1957, began during the Saka Era.

  • 1. Dynasty Founder: The Saka Era is thought to have been founded by King Shalivanhana of the Shatavahana dynasty.

2. Chaitra Month: Chaitra, the first month of the Saka Samvat, begins on March 22, which corresponds to March 21 during the leap year.

VIKRAM SAMVAT
  • 1. Era Origin: The Vikram era began 56 years before the Christian era, in around 56 BC, and is still in effect in almost all of India except the Bengal region.
  • 2. Victory Commemoration: According to historians, King Vikramaditya of Ujjain established this era to commemorate his victory over the Saka rulers.
  • 3. Lunar Calendar: It is a lunar calendar based on the Hindu calendar of ancient times.
  • 4. New Year Timing: The new year begins on the first day following the new moon in the month of Chaitra, which corresponds to March–April in the Gregorian calendar.
  • 5. Fortnight Division: Each month is divided into two halves (fortnights) – the bright and dark halves.
  • 6. Adhik Masa Correction: To compensate for the 11-day difference with the solar year, Vikram samvat has 13 months after a cycle of every 3 years and every 5 years, with an extra month added known as Adhik Masa.
SAKA SAMVAT
  • 1. Calendar Origin: In 78 AD, King Shalivahan instituted this calendar form.
  • 2. Saka Era Name: It was also known as the Saka era because Shalivahan belonged to this tribe.
  • 3. Solar-Lunar Nature: The Saka Calendar is both solar and lunar, with lunar months and a solar year, and it has the same number of months as the Vikram epoch did.
  • 4. Year Commencement: The Saka Calendar begins on March 22nd every year, except in Gregorian leap years, when it begins on March 21st.
  • 5. Fixed Month Days: The Saka calendar has a fixed number of days in each month.
  • 6. Year Duration: A saka year is made up of 365 days.
GREGORIAN CALENDAR
  • 1. Christian Basis: This calendar is based on the birthday of Christianity's founder, Jesus Christ.
  • 2. Calendar Implementation: In October 1582, the Gregorian calendar, which was devised as a corrective to the Julian Calendar, was implemented.
  • 3. Papal Naming: This calendar is named after Pope Gregory XIII.
  • 4. Solar Year Duration: It is a solar year that begins on January 1st and lasts 365 days, 5 hours, 48 minutes, and 46 seconds.

National Bird

1. National Bird Designation: The peacock (Pavo cristatus [Linnaeus]) was named the National Bird of India in 1963 because it is the most auspicious animal that embodies our country's fauna and has a long history of religious and legendary significance in Indian culture.

  • 1. National Spirit Embodiment: The Peacock embodies the national spirit whose colours are connected with Indian identity.
  • 2. Indigenous Habitat: Indian peacocks are the swan's size and are indigenous to Nepal, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, Myanmar and India.
  • 3. Pan-India Presence: The peacock bird is found all over the country and is also well known to the common masses of India.
  • 4. Hindu Religious Symbol: It is also used as an image of the god of rain, thunder and war in Hinduism.
  • 5. Wildlife Protection: It has been given complete protection under the Wildlife Protection Act 1972.
  • 6. Sacred Association: They are also considered sacred by the Hindus as it is also linked to Lord Murugan, the son of Lord Shiva.
  • 7. Maurya Dynasty Link: Chandragupta Maurya, who was the founder of the Maurya dynasty, is also considered to be the son of peacock tamers or the 'Mayur Poshikas' in Sanskrit.
  • 8. Habitat Pattern: The peacocks are omnivorous and are usually found in low altitude grasslands, adjacent human habitats and forest plains.
  • 9. Least Concerned Status: The Red List of IUCN categorizes the peacocks under the least concerned category.
National Tree

1. Banyan Tree Identity: The national tree of India is the Banyan tree(Ficus benghalensis), known for its distinct hanging roots and excellent medicinal properties.

  • 1. Strength and Unity: The banyan tree signifies strength and unity and is therefore considered the national tree of India.

  • 2. Sacred Significance: In Hinduism, the Bargad (Banyan) tree is considered pious and is called 'Ashwath Vriksha'.
  • 3. Edible Fruits: Its fruits are edible and nutritious and can relieve skin irritation and swelling.
  • 4. Bleeding Treatment: Its bark and leaf extracts are used to stop bleeding.
  • 5. Diarrhea Remedy: Leaf bud infusion is used to treat chronic diarrhea/dysentery.
  • 6. Shellac Production: Banyan tree is known for making shellac, used as a surface abrasive and glue.
  • 7. Metal Polishing: Its milky white sap is used to polish metals such as brass and copper.
NATIONAL FLAG

1. First Flag Hoisting: The first national flag in India is said to have been hoisted on August 7, 1906, in the Parsee Bagan Square (Green Park) in Calcutta now Kolkata on the anniversary of anti-partition movement.

  • 1. Flag Designer: The flag was designed by SACHINDRA PRASAD BOSE.
  • 2. Tricolour Composition: The flag was composed of three horizontal stripes of red, yellow and green.
  • 3. Second Flag in Paris: Mrs Cama and her band of exiled revolutionaries hoisted the second flag in Paris in 1907 which was made by HEM CHANDRA DAS..
  • 4. Similar to First: This was very similar to the first flag except that the top strip had only one lotus but the Saptarishi was denoted by seven stars.
  • 5. Berlin Display: The flag was also on display at a Berlin socialist conference.
  • 6. Home Rule Flag: During the Home Rule Movement in 1917 Bal Gangadhar Tilak and Mrs. Anne Besant also designed a flag.
  • 7. Flag Arrangement: This flag was arranged alternately with five red and four green horizontal stripes, with seven stars superimposed on them in the Saptarishi configuration.
  • 8. Union Jack Corner: The Union Jack stood in the top left-hand corner (the pole end). There was also a white crescent and star in one corner.
  • 9. Bezwada Flag Design: During the All India Congress Committee meeting in Bezwada Mr Pingley Venkayya design a national flag for Freedom Movement.

FEDERAL
  • 1. Federal Government Definition: K.C. Wheare defines federal government as, an association of states, which has been formed for certain common purposes, but in which the member states retain a large measure of their original independence.
  • 2. Federal Constitution Principle: A federal Constitution envisages a demarcation of governmental functions and powers between the Centre and the regions by the sanction of the Constitution, which is a written document.

WRITTEN CONSTITUTION
  • 1. Constitutional Mandate: A written constitution is mandatory.
  • 2. Federal Prerequisite: Federal system cannot exist without a written constitution.
  • 3. UK Example: UK does not have a written constitution and so it is not a federal country.
RIGID CONSTITUTION
  • 1. Amendment Rigidity: Rigidity in amendment is a distinctive feature of a federal constitution.
  • 2. State Participation: It cannot be amended by the national legislature unilaterally without the participation of the states.
  • 3. US Ratification Standard: In United States, which is a perfect example of classical federalism, no part of the constitution can be amended without the ratification of at least 3/4th of the individual States.
  • 4. Doctrine of Severability: Supreme court held in Kihoto Hollohan vs Zachilhu & Ors that Doctrine of Severability must be applied.
SUPREMACY OF THE CONSTITUTION
  • 1. Suprema Lex: Constitution is suprema lex (supreme law of land) in India.
  • 2. Constitutional Control: Every type of power (legislative, administrative or judicial), irrespective of it being at the centre or the state level is controlled by the Constitution.
  • 3. Article 13(2): Article 13(2) states that the State shall not make any law which takes away or abridges any of the rights guaranteed under Part III of the Constitution.
  • 4. Constitutional Conformity: All laws enacted both at the Centre and the State level ought to be in line with the Constitutiona scheme.
  • 5. Doctrine of Severability
DISTRIBUTION OF POWERS
  • 1. Division of Powers: The pivotal point of a federal constitution is division of powers between the centre and the states.
  • 2. Seventh Schedule Lists: Under the constitution, there is a three-fold distribution of legislative powers between the Union and the states, made by the three Lists in the seventh schedule of the constitution.
  • 3. Derived Powers: Both the centre and the state governments derive their powers from the constitution.
SOVEREIGNTY
  • 1. Sovereign Power: The sovereign power rests with the body that can amend the constitution.
RAJYA SABHA
  • 1. Rajya Sabha Election: The members of Rajya Sabha are elected by the members of state assembly.

UNITARY
  • 1. Unitary State Definition: A unitary state is a sovereign state governed as one single unit in which the central government is supreme and any administrative divisions (sub national units) exercise only powers that the central government chooses to delegate.
  • 2. Central Supremacy: The Constitution provides Central Government with supreme powers and concentrates administrative and financial powers completely in its hands.
  • 1. Union of States: Article 1 which provides that India i.e. Bharat, shall be a Union of States.
  • 2. No Federation Term: The word 'Federation' is nowhere to been mentioned in the Constitution.
  • 3. Article 2 & 3 Powers: Article 2 and 3 of the Constitution, give the power to the Parliament to redraw the political map of India; to create and abolish the states, change the boundaries of the States or even change their names and this can be achieved by simple legislation by way of simple majority in the Parliament and the Constitution only provides for consultation by the Centre of the concerned State.
  • 4. Article 355 Duty: Central government is duty bound under Article 355 to ensure that there is no failure of constitutional machinery in the State and the states are protected from internal disturbance and external aggression and war.
  • 5. Article 356 Power: The Central government has power under Article 356 to impose Presidential rule and it is the duty of the governor who has to make a report to the Centre about the failure of constitutional machinery of the State for political or any other reasons.
APPOINTMENT OF THE GOVERNOR
  • 6. Governor Appointment: Appointment of the Governors of various States is done by the Centre. Governor is the constitutional head of the State and at the same time he is also the representative of the Centre.
  • 7. Discretionary Powers: Governor unlike President enjoys some discretionary powers.
  • 8. Bill Withholding: Governor can withhold a bill for the consideration of the President.
  • 9. Sarkaria Commission: The Sarkaria Commission made certain recommendations regarding the Governors.
  • 10. Bihar Dissolution Case: In Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India, popularly known as Bihar Assembly Dissolution Case where SC raised questions as to the impartiality of the Governor.
  • 11. Governor Removal: In B.P. Singhal v Union of India, SC held that a Governor cannot be removed by the Central government on the grounds that he is not in sync with the policies of the Central government or the ideology of ruling party.
  • 12. Key Position Appointments: Appointment on Key Positions such as the Chief Election Commissioner, the Comptroller and Auditor General are made by the Union Government and All India Services such as IAS and IPS have been created which are kept under the control of the Union.

EMERGENCY
  • 1. Emergency Power: Only the Centre has the power to impose emergency under Articles 352, 356 and 360. Emergency under Article 352 can be imposed only when the nation is threatened by external aggression or armed rebellion.
  • 2. S.R. Bommai Guidelines: The Supreme Court held in S.R. Bommai case laid down guidelines for the implementation of Article 356.
POWER TO MAKE LAWS
  • 1. Article 249: Article 249 gives the Parliament the power to make laws under the State list. Such law can also be legislated at a special request of a group of states. Also during President's Rule in a state all the bills pending in the dissolved and the State
SINGLE CITIZENSHIP
  • 1. Single Citizenship: Indians have only one citizenship unlike the citizens of the United States of America.
ARMED FORCES
  • 1. Armed Forces Deployment: The Armed Forces can be deployed in the States at the Centre's will without the consultation of the State Government.
  • 2. AFSPA: AFSPA—Armed Forces Special Powers Act.
RESIDUARY POWERS
  • 1. Article 248: Residuary Powers (Article 248) allows the Centre to make laws on subjects not mentioned in List II This is enjoyed by the Centre only.
SINGLE INTEGRATED COURTS
  • 1. Unified Judiciary: In India we have Unified Judiciary with the Supreme Court at the apex as opposed to the federal system having a dual system of courts.
  • 2. Supreme Court Apex: The Supreme Court occupies the highest place in our unitary judicial system.
  • 3. Unequal Representation: Representation in the Legislature, which is equal in case of a true federation such as United States, is not applicable in case of Indian States.
  • 4. Rajya Sabha Representation: Representation of States in Rajya Sabha is not equal.
CONCLUSION
  • 1. Kuldip Nair Case: The Supreme Court held in Kuldip Nair v Union of India that a particular kind of federalism or a US type of model may not be part of the basic structure of Indian Constitution.
  • 2. Unique Federalism: The Indian Federalism is unique in nature and is tailored according to the specific needs of the country.
  • 3. Basic Feature: Federalism is a basic feature of the Constitution of India in which the Union of India is permanent and indestructible.
  • 4. Quasi-Federal Nature: According to KC Wheare, in practice, the Constitution of India is quasi-federal in nature and not strictly federal.
  • 5. S.R. Bommai Observation: In S.R. Bommai v Union of India, Justice Ahmadi opined that the essence of federation is the existence of distribution of power between the Union and the States.
  • 6. Quasi-Federal Conclusion: It can help us conclude that the Constitution of India is more 'quasi federal' than 'federal' or 'unitary'.
  • 7. Amphibian Constitution: Former Chief Justice Beg, called the Constitution of India as 'amphibian'.
  • 8. Shamsher Singh Case: The Supreme Court held in Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab, that the constitution was 'more unitary than federal.

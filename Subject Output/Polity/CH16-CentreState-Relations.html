CENTRE – STATE RELATIONS
ARTICLE 245
  • 1. Territorial Jurisdiction: (1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, Parliament may make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India, and the Legislature of a State may make laws for the whole or any part of the State.
  • 2. Extraterritorial Operation: (2) No law made by Parliament shall be deemed to be invalid on the ground that it would have extraterritorial operation.
ARTICLE 246
  • 1. Union List: (1) Parliament has exclusive power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I (Union List) in the Seventh Schedule.
  • 2. Concurrent List: (2) Parliament and the Legislature of any State also have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List III (Concurrent List) in the Seventh Schedule.
  • 3. Centre's Supremacy: But the Centre has a prior and supreme claim to legislate on concurrent subjects. In case of conflict between the law of the State and Union law on a subject in the Concurrent list, the law of the Parliament prevails.
  • 4. State List: (3) The Legislature of any State has exclusive power to make laws for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II (State List) in the Seventh Schedule.
ARTICLE 248

1. Residuary Powers: The residuary powers have been granted to the Union contrary to the convention in other federations of the world, where the residuary powers are given to the States.

2. Judicial Review: In case of any conflict, whether a particular matter falls under the residuary power or not is to be decided by the court.

PARLIAMENT'S POWER TO LEGISLATE ON STATE LIST

1. Special Conditions: Though under ordinary circumstances the Central Government does not possess power to legislate on subjects enumerated in the State List, but under certain special conditions the Union Parliament can make laws even on these subjects.

ARTICLE 249
  • 1. National Interest: In the national interest, if the Rajya Sabha declares by a resolution supported by not less than 2/3 of its members present and voting, that it is necessary or expedient in the national interest that Parliament should make laws with respect to any matter enumerated in the State List.
  • 2. Territorial Scope: After such a resolution is passed, Parliament can make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India. Such a resolution remains in force for a period of 1 year and can be further extended by one year by means of a subsequent resolution.
  • 3. Duration Limit: (2) A resolution passed under clause (1) shall remain in force for such period not exceeding one year.
ARTICLE 250

1. National Emergency: Parliament can legislate on the subjects mentioned in the State List when the Proclamation of National Emergency is in operation. However, the laws made by Parliament under this provision shall cease to have effect on the expiration of a period of six months after the Proclamation has ceased to operate, except as respects things done or omitted to be done before the expiry of the said period.

ARTICLE 252

1. Agreement Between States: Parliament can also legislate on a State subject if the legislatures of two or more states resolve that it is lawful for Parliament to make laws with respect to any matter enumerated in the State List relating to those States. Any Act passed by Parliament shall apply to such states and to any other state which passes such a resolution. Parliament also reserves the right to amend or repeal any such act.

ARTICLE 253

1. Implement Treaties: Parliament can make law for the whole or any part of the territory of India for implementing any treaty, international agreement or convention with any other country. Any law passed by Parliament for this purpose cannot be invalidated on the ground that it relates to the subject mentioned in the State list.

COOPERATIVE FEDERALISM IS PROMOTING HEALTHY CENTRE STATE RELATIONS

1. Federal Philosophy: Federalism is derived from the Latin word foedus, which means agreement or treaty. Thus, it is a philosophy of political organization that involves a combination of the principles of centralization, non-centralization, and power-sharing. Sardar Patel was strongly an advocate of the federal system and played a crucial role in crafting a federal constitution.

2. S.R. Bommai Case: The Supreme Court held in the S.R. Bommai case, the States are not mere appendages of the Union and the latter should ensure that the powers of the States are not trampled with.

3. Cooperative Federalism: Federal India is no longer a hindrance for better centre-state relations but rather it has given new hope to the mixed efforts of the Centre and states which may be referred to as cooperative federalism. The Indian Constitution already distinguishes the authority of the Centre and the states. The provision for separate lists reduces misunderstanding and disagreement between the Centre and the states. There would be less confusion and more amicable interactions between the Center and the states (Art. 246).

Page 58

1. NITI Aayog: Replacing the older Planning Commission where only the Union had a say, NITI Aayog boosts the morale of the states and holds them responsible for better decision making since they are now a part of the process.

2. Dual GST Model: In 2017, India adopted a dual-structure GST model wherein both the Centre as well as the states have an important role. Both are empowered to levy and collect taxes.

3. State Governance: States play a crucial role since they can manage the situation and rule the region in accordance with the demands of the population, which will ultimately result in the inclusion of additional groups of people in mainstream society.

4. Efficient Administration: A federal system of government divides authority between the central government and the states, which facilitates efficient administration. Additionally, the states would be better equipped to control the problems associated with it.

5. Trust & Faith: This way, a better relation of trust and faith can be witnessed between the Centre and the states.

ARTICLE 246 IS THE CORNERSTONE OF CENTRE STATE LEGISLATIVE RELATIONS

1. Division of Powers: The essential principles guiding the division of powers between the Central and state governments are outlined in Part XI of Article 246 of the Indian Constitution. The purpose of the Constitution is to grant a constitutionally powerful Centre with sufficient powers, both in terms of scope and nature, to protect and uphold the nation's unity and integrity. The Indian Constitution aims to create three distinct functional areas:

  • 2. Centre's Area: An exclusive area for the Centre;
  • 3. States' Area: An exclusive area for the States; and
  • 4. Concurrent Area: A shared or concurrent area in which the Centre and the States may coexist, subject to the Centre's ultimate supremacy.

5. Historical Basis: The Union was given larger powers relative to US, due to historical reasons, partition and to control certain separatist tendencies and for equal development of all regions.

6. Union List: According to Article 246(1), Parliament has the "exclusive power" to enact laws regarding any item on the Union List. This List includes entries such as the necessity for a uniform law for the entire country. The states have no authority to enact legislation in this area.

7. State List: Article 246 (3) gives the states the exclusive power to make laws concerning the items included in the State List. These are issues that allow for local variances and are best handled at the state level from an administrative standpoint, hence, the Centre is barred from legislating on these issues.

8. Concurrent List: This division of powers has helped in clarity of domains and effective exercise by the Union and states while the provision in concurrent lists give flexibility and initiative to either sides for judgement of situation and legislation in issues that have both a national and local connotation.

PROCLAMATION OF NATIONAL EMERGENCY AFFECTS CENTRE STATE RELATIONS

1. Changed Relations: The normal fabric of the Centre-state relations undergoes a basic change during national emergency.

  • 2. Executive Directions: The Centre becomes entitled to give executive directions on any matter. Thus, the state governments are brought under the complete control of the Centre, though they are not suspended.
Page 59
  • 1. Parliamentary Power: The Parliament becomes empowered to make laws on any subject mentioned in the state list. Although the legislative power of state legislature is not suspended.
  • 2. Presidential Ordinance: President can issue ordinances on the state subject, if the Parliament is not in session.
  • 3. Unitary Character: The Constitution becomes unitary than federal.
  • 4. Revenue Modification: The president can modify, reduce or cancel, the constitutional distribution of revenues between the Centre and the states.
  • 5. Financial Year Limit: Such modification continues till the end of the financial year in which the emergency is revoked.

6. Necessary Evil: During National emergency time the Centre becomes all powerful. The Constitution becomes unitary rather than federal without even amending the Constitution. However these powers are a necessary evil to enable central government to meet any abnormal situations effectively.

THE ROLE OF THE FINANCE COMMISSION IN MAINTAINING FISCAL FEDERALISM IN INDIA

1. Constitutional Mandate: The Finance Commission (Art. 280) is a Constitutionally mandated body that is at the centre of fiscal federalism by performing following functions -

  • 2. Tax Proceeds: The distribution between the Union and the States of the net proceeds of taxes which are to be divided between them and the allocation between the States of the respective shares of such proceeds.
  • 3. Grants-in-Aid: Determination of principles and quantum of grants-in-aid to States which are in need of such assistance.
  • 4. Local Body Funds: Measures needed to augment the Consolidated Fund of a State to supplement the resources of the Panchayats and Municipalities in the State on the basis of the recommendations made by the Finance Commission of the State.

5. Permanent Status: Despite the changing fiscal environment and considerable expansion in its functions, the institution of the Finance Commission has ensured smooth functioning of Centre-State fiscal relations. Yet the demand to accord permanent status to the Finance Commission seems right on the following grounds-

  • 6. Fiscal Consolidation: There is a need for robust expenditure planning without compromising on fiscal consolidation as fiscal federalism gathers momentum in the era of uniform Goods and Services tax (GST).
  • 7. Fund Flow Certainty: There is a need to ensure consistency between Finance Commissions so that there is some certainty in the flow of funds to States.
  • 8. Implementation Issues: During the intervening period, it can also address issues arising from implementation of the recommendation of the Commission.
  • 9. Fiscal Prudence: This will also help add knowledge and capacities and boost fiscal prudence. The upcoming Finance Commission will be able to get a clear hearing on the challenges and rate of effectiveness of its likely recommendations.

  • 1. Leaner Entity: The Commission can function as a leaner entity in the intervening period till the next Finance Commission is set up in a full-fledged manner.
  • 2. Apolitical Body: There is considerable merit in having one apolitical body that provides stability and predictability primarily to share taxes that ensures fiscal balance and to refocus the scope of the Finance Commission to maintain the trust of all stakeholders in the institution.

THE ISSUES OF COOPERATIVE FEDERALISM

1. Centre-State Cooperation: Cooperative federalism is the concept which reflects the relationship between centre and state where they both come together and resolve the common problems with each other's cooperation. It is the horizontal relationship between union and states and shows neither is above the other.

  • 2. Inter-State Council: The Inter-State Council as mentioned in Article 263 is supposed to be a body for intergovernmental consultation and co-operation. But it has not been given the powers to inquire and advise on disputes between States.
  • 3. Water Disputes: Article 262 that provides for the resolution of inter-state water disputes which also failed to contain many disputes which reached it despite repeated hearings and decisions. Failure of this can be because of both structural and procedural inadequacies.
  • 4. Governor's Appointment: This has been evident since long that as the political party gets changed in the centre the Governors appointed by the earlier party were asked to resign. Governor's appointment should not be politically motivated.
  • 5. 7th Schedule Reform: The 7th Schedule needs to be reformed to ensure greater empowerment of States in accordance with increased financial transfers characteristic of cooperative federalism.
  • 6. Rajya Sabha Equality: Equal representation of states in Rajya Sabha is another blow to the cooperative federalism. There should be equal representation as there is in United States of America.
  • 7. Presidential Assent: State bills which are reserved for President assent should be disposed of as early as possible. This creates impediment in the growth of the country and ultimately it hits the cooperation between centre and state.
  • 8. NITI Aayog Flaws: Though, NITI Aayog is established with the aim to foster the cooperative federalism but if we look at the structure and function of it, it is flawed in many ways. The function and jurisdiction of NITI Aayog is not specifically laid down.
  • 9. International Treaties: International treaties which are greatly overlooked the involvement of the states, it should be strengthened as while negotiating the international treaties involvement of states should be there with greater extent as its implementing will surely going to impact on the states.

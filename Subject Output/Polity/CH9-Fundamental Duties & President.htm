AS A TITULAR HEAD

1. Real Position: The real position of the President of India is clouded with different suspects as a mere figure head, titular head, rubber stamp or golden zero.

  • 2. Head of State: The President is the head of the State, not of the Government.
  • 3. Article 53 Provision: Article 53 clearly states that "the executive power of the Union shall be vested in the President which means that the Parliament has the power to confer only the functions of the President on any other authorities and not the powers.
  • 4. Not Presidential System: Does not have the Presidential form of Government.
  • 5. British Tradition: British tradition still prevails in our constitution.
  • 6. Titular Head Status: The last but not the least is that the President is Constitutionally bound to act on the advise of the Council of Ministers and has few discretionary powers, he/she is referred to as the titular head of the State.

7. 42nd Amendment: The Constitution 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 provided in article 74 that "there shall be a Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister at the head to aid and advise the President who shall, in the exercise of his functions, act in accordance with such advice." That is how the rubber stamp-theory gained currency.

8. 44th Amendment: By the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978 provided that the President may require the Council of Ministers to reconsider such advice, either generally or otherwise, and the President shall act in accordance with the advice tendered after such reconsideration.

DISCRETIONARY POWERS

1. De Jure Head: The President is the De Jure head of the state therefore it is his responsibility to preserve, protect and defend the Constitution of India. He is not a ceremonial head so all important decisions are taken in the name of the President, though most of these will be based on the binding advice given by Council of Ministers.

  • 2. Pocket Veto: The President is at his discretion to give his assent, or withhold his assent for an indefinite period or return the Bill to the House for reconsideration (except in case of a money bill). The power of pocket veto (article 111) was used by former President Giani Zail Singh in 1986 when he refused to grant assent to the Post Office (Amendment) Bill.
  • 3. Article 78 Power: Article 78 of the Constitution empowers the President to seek information regarding the affairs of the Union Government from the Prime Minister.
  • 4. Government Formation: When no political party or a coalition of parties assumes a majority in the Lok Sabha post general elections, he may exercise his discretion in selecting someone who in his opinion will be able to form a stable government.
  • 5. Dinesh Chandra Case: The Supreme Court held in Dinesh Chandra case that to argue pleasure could be interpreted in Art.75(2) to mean the President can dismiss any minister at any time, at his will.
  • 6. PM Succession: When a Prime Minister in office dies suddenly and there is no successor to replace him, the President has to take charge in the selection and appointment of the next Prime Minister (Rajeev Gandhi by Zail Singh).
  • 7. Caretaker Government: The president wields a major responsibility in taking important administrative decisions in the governance of a caretaker government.
  • 8. House Dissolution: When a Council of Ministers loses majority in the Lok Sabha, it is the discretion of the President to decide if the house should be dissolved or not.
  • 9. Summoning Houses: According to Article 85, the President enjoys the discretion of summoning and proroguing both the Houses.

10. Executive Check: The discretionary powers vested with the President empower him with the authority to check the arbitrariness of the executive and wield a substantial amount of control over them thereby protecting the interests of the vast majority in the country and fulfilling the responsibility of a true leader.

IMMUNITIES TO THE PRESIDENT (ARTICLE - 361)
  • 1. Good Faith Actions: The actions taken in good faith, in exercise of powers or performance of functions, by the President can not be challenged in any court.
  • 2. Civil Suit Immunity: No civil suit can be instituted against him in any court either in personal capacity or in official capacity.
  • 3. Criminal Immunity: The President is not liable for any criminal proceedings in existing matters or in future matter during he holds the office of the President.
  • 4. Writ Immunity: No writ of Mandamus or Quo Warranto can be issued against him.

DISTINCTION BETWEEN INDIAN PRESIDENT AND AMERICAN PRESIDENT

1. Comparative Overview: Apparently there seems to be much in common between the Presidents of India and USA. Both are elected heads of their respective. But in fact there is nothing in common between the two except their names.

  • 2. Head of State vs Govt: The President of India is Head of the State, but not the head of Government while the US President is that the head of state and mayor of state.
  • 3. Election Method: US president is more or less directly elected whereas the Indian President is indirectly elected. The advantage of the directly elected head of the government is the stability of the government.
  • 4. Council vs Cabinet: The Council of Ministers is more powerful than the President while in US the members of the cupboard are chosen by the President and they are called secretaries not Ministers.
  • 5. Binding Advice: The President of India is sure to accept the recommendation of the Council of Ministers. He has the power to return its advice to the Council of Ministers for reconsideration. But he is bound to accept it, if the same advice is sent back to him after reconsideration by the Council of Ministers. The US President is Boss and Secretaries are his subordinates and he is not sure to stick on the recommendation of the cupboard and have perfect veto.
  • 6. Separation of Powers: Doctrine of Separation of Powers strictly appealed in America. The President is not a member of legislature. It is the responsibility of the Congress to make the laws.
  • 7. Re-election Limit: American President can only be elected twice where as Indian can be chosen multiple times.
  • 8. Impeachment Ground: Indian President can only be impeached only for violation of constitution where as the US President can be impeached for misbehaviour too.

9. Plenitude of Power: Thus it is said that the American President exercises " The largest amount of authority ever wielded by any man in a democracy". The Indian President has no such plenitude of powers in actual fact.

4. Ambedkar's Vision: Dr. Ambedkar envisaged it to remain a 'dead letter' in the constitution.

5. Bommai Case Ruling: A nine-judge Constitution Bench of the court in the Bommai case, which is the reigning authority on the use and misuse of Article 356, had held that political whim or fancy cannot form the basis for the President to proclaim central rule in a State.

6. Parliamentary Pre-condition: The President should exercise the power only after his proclamation (imposing his/her rule) is approved by both Houses of Parliament. Till then the President can only suspend the Legislative Assembly by suspending the provisions of Constitution relating to the Legislative Assembly.

7. Objective Material Test: It is not the personal whim, wish, view or opinion of the President de hors the material, but a legitimate inference drawn from the material placed before him which is relevant for the purpose. Such objective material may be available in the report sent to the President by the Governor or otherwise or both from the report and other sources.

  • 8. Judicial Review Scope: The court had stated that although the sufficiency or otherwise of the material cannot be questioned, the legitimacy of inference drawn from such material is "certainly open to judicial review".

9. Ministerial Advice: The proclamation by the President under Article 356 is on the advice of the Council of Ministers tendered under Article 74(1). The judgment had explained that in a multi-party political system, chances are high that the political parties in the Centre and the State concerned may not be the same. Article 356 cannot be used for the purpose of political one-upmanship by the Centre.

10. Framers' Intent: That is why the framers of the Constitution had taken pains to specify that President should proclaim central rule in a State only, and only, if a situation arises by which governance of the State is either disabled or prevented from continuing "in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution".

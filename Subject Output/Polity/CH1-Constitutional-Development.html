The Regulating Act, 1773

The Regulating Act of 1773 was the first direct interference of the Home Government in the Company's affairs which brought the major changes in the Company's administration.

Need

After the Treaty of Allahabad in 1765 Diarchy was imposed in Bengal by Robert Clive. This system created so many complication in the administration of Bengal.

  • 1. Nizamat Rights: Nizamat rights were given to Company.
  • 2. Nawab as Agent: Nawab acted as an agent of the company.
  • 3. Financial Crisis: Due to financial crisis the company had demanded a loan of one million pounds from the Home Government in the year 1772.
  • 4. Ruling Body: The Company acted like a ruling body.
  • 5. Severe Famine: People of Bengal were perished due to severe famine.
  • 6. Corruption: Employees of Company involved in corruption.
  • 7. No Centralized Authority: There was no centralized authority to control the presidencies of Bengal, Madras, and Bombay.

The British Parliament passed two bills presented by Prime Minister Lord North in 1772 after the recommendation of secret committee. The second bill was known as Regulating Act 1773.

Objectives
  • 1. Control Company Affairs: To control and regulate the affairs of the Company
  • 2. Central Administration: To provide a Central Administration System
  • 3. Control Corruption: To control the corruption practices by
    • a. Private Trade Ban: Prohibiting the servants of the company from engaging in private trade
    • b. Bribes Ban: Prohibiting the servants of the company from accepting bribes, gifts, and presents from the people
  • 4. Diarchy Issues: To sort out the issues of the Diarchy
Page 2
Features
  • 1. Governor-General Office: The office of the Governor of Bengal was re-designated as the Office of the Governor-General of Bengal.
    • a. First Governor-General: Lord Warren Hastings was the first Governor - General of Bengal.
  • 2. Executive Council: The Executive Council of four members was created to assist the Governor-General.
    • a. Council Members: John Clavering, George Monson, Richard Barwell & Philip Francis were the members.
  • a. Tenure: The tenure of the members was five years.
  • b. Crown Appointment: Crown had right to appoint them or remove them.
  • 3. Directors' Term: The term of directors was increased from one year to four years and provision was made for electing one fourth of the directors every year in rotation.
  • 4. Voting Power: Voting power of the shareholders were restricted to those who held stock worth 1000 Pounds or more in the company.
  • 5. Subordination of Governors: The Governors of Bombay and Madras were made subordinate to the Governor General of Bengal.
  • 6. Supreme Court: In the year 1774, Supreme Court was established at Fort William Calcutta with one Chief Justice and three other judges.
    • a. First Chief Justice: The first Chief Justice of the court was Sir Elijah Impey.
    • b. Three Judges: Three judges were John Hyde, Robert Chambers & Stephen Caesar.
  • a. Authority of Judges: The authority of these judges was like those of King's Bench in England.
  • b. Salary Fixed: The salary of the Chief Justice was fixed at 8000 sterling a year, and of three judges 6000 sterling each.
  • 7. Revenue Issues: All the revenue related issues were presented before the revenue department of home government.
Impacts
  • 1. Judiciary Structure: It brought a significant changes in the structure of judiciary in India.
  • 2. Court of Directors: It changed the Constitution of Court of Directors.
  • 3. Company Functions: The political and administrative functions of the company were recognized.
  • 4. Central Administration: The act laid down the foundation of the Central Administration in the country.
Demerits
  • 1. No Veto Power: The Governor-General had no veto power.
  • 2. Answerable to Directors: The Governor-General was made answerable to the Directors and was held responsible for all the acts related to the administration in India.
  • 3. Puppet of Council: The council Governor-General became the puppet of the council.
  • 4. Jurisdictional Confusion: There was a lot of confusion between the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and that of the Council of the Governor-General.
  • 5. Failed Corruption Control: It was failed to control the corruption.
  • 6. Indian Natives Ignored: The act failed to address the concerns of the Indian natives.
Page 3
  • 7. Presidency Confusion: There was a lot of confusion in relationship between the Governor-General and Governors of Presidency.
    • a. Anglo-Maratha War: Reflected at the time of first Anglo-Maratha war.
  • 8. Administration Issues: Serious issues with the administration
    • a. Nand Kumar Case: Nand Kumar case in which he was hanged.

Charter Act of 1813
Cause

The English traders wanted to compensate their loss due to the Continental System of Napoleon Bonaparte by sharing the Indian Trade.

Due to the Industrial revolution the production was also increased in Britain. Therefore the British trader demanded the British government to permit them for trade with India.

  • 1. Commercial Monopoly Ended: The Company was deprived of its commercial monopoly.
  • 2. Crown Sovereignty: Laid down 'The Undoubted Sovereignty of the Crown'.
  • 3. China and Tea Monopoly: The East India Company was allowed to enjoy the monopoly of China trade and trade in tea.
  • 4. Education Fund: A sum of rupees one lakh annually was provided for the improvement of Education. (First step towards the principle of State responsibility for education.)
  • 5. Bishop Appointment: Appointment of a BISHOP whose headquarter was in CALCUTTA.
  • 6. Christian Missionaries: India was to be open to the Christian missionaries.
Charter Act of 1833
Cause

Starting of Machine Age in Britain.

The Whigs government accepted the Laissez Faire policy.

  • 1. Twenty Year Lease: It gave another lease of life for twenty years to the East India Company.
  • 2. Political Organisation: The Company's commercial privileges were all abolished now it worked as a political organisation.
  • 3. Minister for Indian Affairs: The President of the Board of Control became Minister for Indian affairs.
  • 4. Revenue Powers: The Council got full powers regarding revenue.
    • a. Single Budget: A single budget was prepared by the Governor General for the country.
  • 5. Governor-General of India: The Governor-General of Bengal was now made the Governor-General of India.
    • a. First Governor-General: Lord WILLIAM BENTINCK was the first governor general of British India.

    • a. First Law Member: Lord MACAULAY was the first Law Member.

  • 8. No Distinction: It removed the distinction between Indian and European.
    • a. No Disqualification: No one would be disqualified for any place in the Company's service by reason of his religion, place of birth, descent, or colour.

Charter Act of 1853
  • 1. No Time Limit: No specific time was allotted to the Company to retain its powers and authority in India.
  • 2. Directors Reduced: The number of the Directors of the Court of Directors was reduced from 24 to 18 of whom 6 were to be nominated by the Crown.
  • 3. Salary of Board Members: The Salary of the members of Board of Control, its Secretary and other officers would be fixed by the British Government and paid by the Company's funds.
  • 4. Macaulay Committee: MACAULAY COMMITTEE was setup to give effect to the scheme of appointment of civil servant through competition.
  • 5. Full Member Status: Law member was now made a full member of the Governor-General's Executive Council.
  • 6. Membership Enlarged: The Governor-General's Executive Council's membership was enlarged by inclusion of six members.
  • 7. Civil Servants Appointed: The Governor-General was empowered to appoint two civil servants to his Council.
  • 8. Legislative Council Created: It created a functioning Legislative Council.
Government of India Act 1858

Indian alliance were defeated by the East India Company force in the 1857 Revolution. The British government decided to make Indian territory which was controlled by the East India Company as the colony of the Britain. Therefore the Act was passed in 1858 in British Parliament also known as Queens declaration.

At that time QUEEN VICTORIA ruled over the British Empire.

  • 1. Palmerston's Bill: Lord PALMERSTON introduced the bill for the Better Government of India in February, 1858.
    • a. Ministry Ousted: His Ministry was thrown out.
  • 2. Act Passed: The Act was passed under DISRAELI ministry and received royal assent on August 2, 1858.
  • 3. British Queen Rule: The Britain Indian territories were now governed by the British Queen.
  • 4. Parliamentary Control: It established the control of British Parliament over Indian affairs.
  • 5. Secretary of State: Secretary of State for India assisted by a Council of 15 members.
    • a. Council Composition: 8 were to be appointed by the Crown and 7 were elected by the Court of Directors.
Page 6
  • 1. Service Requirement: At least 50 per cent of the members of the Council must have served in India for not less than 10 years.
  • 6. Secretary of State Powers: The Secretary of State for India was empowered with all powers, exercised by the Court of Directors and the Board of control.
    • a. First Secretary of State: The first Secretary of State for India was Lord STANLEY.
  • 7. Governor-General and Viceroy: The representative of the British government in India was the Governor-General and Viceroy (both the same person to avoid conflict).
    • a. First Viceroy: First Governor-General and Viceroy of India was Lord CANNING.
  • 8. Executive Council Members: The Governor General Executive Council had 6-12 members, of which at least half could be Indian or English.
  • 9. Doctrine of Lapse Ended: It ended the doctrine of lapse.
  • 10. Magna Carta: The act was called the Magna Carta.

After the Queen's declaration of 1858, India became the colony of Britain and governed by the British Parliament. Therefore all the Acts related to India were passed by the British government and known as Government of India Act.

Indian Council Act, 1861
The Executive Council of the Governor-General
  • 1. Additional Members: Strengthened by the addition of not less than 6 and not more than 12 members nominated by the Governor-General for the purposes of legislation.
    • a. Non-Officials: Not less than half of the additional members were to be Non-Officials and they were to hold office for two years.
  • 2. Fifth Member: Addition of a FIFTH member to the Executive Council of the Viceroy.
    • a. Jurist Required: He was required to be a jurist rather than a technical lawyer.
  • 3. Limited to Legislation: The function of the Council was strictly limited to Legislation.
  • 4. Home Government Authority: No law could be made which infringed the authority of the Home Government.
  • 5. Veto Power: Governor-General was given the power of veto.
Powers of the Governor-General
  • 1. Ordinance Power: Empowered to issue ORDINANCES in case of emergency.
    • a. Ordinance Duration: These ordinances were to remain in force for SIX months unless they were dissolved or repealed by the ordinance or law.
  • 2. Portfolio System: Empowered to delegate special business to individual members of the Executive Council.
    • a. Portfolio Started: PORTFOLIOS system was started by Lord CANNING.
  • 3. Rules and Regulations: Empowered to make rules and regulations for the conduct of the business of the Executive Council.
  • 4. Province Creation: Empowered to create new provinces, divide or alter the limits of any province.
The Governments of Bombay and Madras

  • 4. Advocate General: Nominating the Advocate General.
Other Facts
  • 1. No Subject Distinction: No distinction was made between the central and provincial subjects.
  • 2. Nominated Non-Officials: The non-official members of the Council were nominated by the Viceroy and not elected by the people.
    • a. Type of Members: These non-official members were either the Indian Princes or their Diwans, big Zamindars or retired officials. First three Indians nominated in Council -

Indian Council Act, 1909

(Minto-Morely Reforms)

The Indian Council act 1909 was the response and reaction of the political condition prevailing in India after the partition of Bengal. The Indians had lost faith in the British government.

So to counter the growing reaction of the Nationalist British government felt the need of the reform.

It was also enacted to attack Hindu Muslim Unity.

  • a. Viceroy and Secretary: Lord Minto and Lord John Morley, were the Viceroy and Secretary of State of British India respectively.
Features
Page 9
  • 1. First Indian Member: Lord Minto appointed Satyendra P Sinha as the first Indian member of the Viceroy's Executive Council.
  • 2. Indians in Secretary's Council: Two Indians were nominated to the Council of the Secretary of State for Indian affairs.
  • 3. Imperial Legislative Council: The Imperial Legislative Council shall consist of 37 officials and 23 non officials.
    • a. Official Members: Out of the 37 officials, 28 were nominated by the Governor-General and the rest were to be ex-officio members.
    • b. Non-Official Members: Out of the 23 non-official members, 5 were to be nominated by the Governor-General and the rest were to be elected.
  • 4. Provincial Councils: The Act did not provide for any official majority in the Provincial Legislative Councils.
    • a. Non-Official Majority: The majority of the members were to be non officials.
    • b. Governor's Nominees: Some of the non-officials were to be nominated by the Governor.
  • 5. Indirect Election: The members were elected INDIRECTLY.
    • a. Electoral College: The local bodies elected an electoral college who would elect members of the provincial legislative councils.
    • b. Central Council Election: These elected members would elect the members of the Central legislative council.
    • c. Elected Categories: The elected members were from the local bodies, the Chambers of Commerce, Landlords, Universities, Traders' communities and Muslims.
  • 6. Resolution on Grants: Every number was given the right to move any resolution grant to local Governments proposed or mentioned in the financial statement.
  • 7. Expenditure Restrictions: The Council was not permitted to discuss expenditure on interest of debt, expenditure and State Railways.
  • 8. Supplementary Questions: The members were given the right to ask question and supplementary questions.
  • 9. Separate Electorates: The act provides for Separate electorates for the Muslims.
    • a. Reserved Seats: Some seats were reserved for Muslims and only Muslims could vote there.

The act did not full fill the aspirations of Indians. They had lost faith in British government.

  • a. No Budget Vote: Indian were not allowed to vote on budget.
  • b. Divisive Attempt: It was an attempt to divide Indian among religion lines.
  • c. Adult Suffrage Denied: Indian were demanding adult suffrage but it was not fulfilled.

According to Sri Ram Sharma, "The constitutional autocracy that it set up was bound to find its autocratic side challenged by its constitutionalism, and an answer had to be found for that challenge. Autocracy was likely to go overboard."

According to Lord Morley, these reforms are as "the opening of a very important chapter in the history of relations of Great Britain and India" and "the turning over a fresh leaf in the history of British responsibility to India".

The reforms of 1909 gave to the people of the country a shadow rather than substance.

Page 10

The British government wanted to suppress the emergence of nationalists because they became increasingly vocal and adopted a stronger tone while demanding representation of Indians in government. The extremist also aimed to undermine the foundations of the British rule.

Therefore the British termed the prevailing political situation in the country as the Indian Unrest. Lord Minto denounced the extremists but felt it was imperative to engage with the moderates and provide some political concessions which came to be packaged as the Morley-Minto reforms.

The reforms of 1909 afforded no answer and could afford no answer to the Indian political problem. Lord Morley made it clear that colonial self-government was not suitable for India, and he was against introduction of parliamentary or responsible government in India.

  • 1. Separate Electorates Impact: The introduction of separate electorates on the basis of religion was viewed as divisive. In Indian politics, this system ushered in an era of unabashed communalism. This had serious consequences which impacted the nation's unity and the freedom struggle.
  • 2. Limited Franchise: It also disappointed in term of limited franchise and unreasonable qualifications required to stand for elections.
  • 3. Dividing Nationalist Ranks: The 'constitutional' reforms were, in fact, aimed at dividing the nationalist ranks by confusing the Moderates and at checking the growth of unity among Indians through the obnoxious instrument of separate electorates.
  • 4. Non-Official Majority Invalidated: Although the Provincial Councils had a non-official majority, the outcome was irrelevant because the non-official majority was invalidated by the election of nominated members.
  • 5. Budget Limitations: Members were able to discuss the budget, but they were unable to make any significant changes to it. They could ask questions but not compel the executives to respond to the resolutions, which were more like recommendations to the government.
  • 6. Diverted Attention: Morley-Minto Reform diverted attention away from political and economic difficulties that afflicted all Indians, Hindu and Muslim alike.
  • 7. No Significant Change: It did not bring any significant change in the power of the councils. The autocratic form of government which came up after the revolt of 1857 remained unchanged.

It was instituted to placate the moderates and appeasement to the disseminate Muslims from National Movement by granting them separate electorate. The people had demanded self-government but what they were given was 'benevolent despotism'.

Indian Council Act, 1919

(MONTAGU-CHELMSFORD REFORMS)

The act was based on the recommendations of a report by Edwin Montagu(Secretary of State for India) and Lord Chelmsford(Viceroy).

It was a step in to introduce Responsible Government system in India as an integral part of Empire.

Diarchy in Provincial Government
Page 11
  • 1. Subject Classification: Classification of the central and provincial subjects.
  • 2. Provincial Division: The provincial subjects were divided into two groups —
    • a. Reserved: Reserved subjects — justice, police, revenue.
    • b. Transferred: Transferred subjects — public health, public works, education etc.
  • 3. Reserved Charge: The governor was in charge of the reserved list along with his executive councillors.
  • 4. Transferred Charge: The ministers were in charge of subjects under the transferred list.
EXECUTIVE IN PROVINCE
  • 1. Minister Responsibility: The ministers were responsible to the people who elected them through the legislature.
  • 2. Minister Nomination: These ministers were nominated from among the elected members of the legislative council.
  • 3. Councillor Immunity: The executive councillors were not responsible to the legislature unlike the ministers.
  • 4. Elected Members: 70% of the members were elected in provincial legislative assemblies.
  • 5. Electorate Type: There were communal and class electorates.
  • 6. Women Suffrage: Some women could also vote.
CENTRAL GOVERNMENT
  • 1. Bicameral Legislature: A bicameral legislature was set up with two houses —
    • a. Legislative Assembly: Legislative Assembly (Lok Sabha)
    • b. Council of State: Council of State (Rajya Sabha)
LEGISLATIVE ASSEMBLY
  • 1. Composition: Legislative Assembly included 145 members —
    • a. Nominated: 41 were nominated (26 official and 15 non-official)
    • b. Elected: 104 were elected (General, Muslims, Europeans, Landlords, representatives of India community, and Sikhs)
  • 2. Nominated Source: The nominated members were nominated by the Governor-General from Anglo-Indians and Indian Christians.
  • 3. Tenure: The members had a tenure of 3 years.
COUNCIL OF STATE
  • 1. Composition: Council of State included 60 male members —
    • a. Nominated: 27 nominated which consisted of official, non-official members.
    • b. Elected: 33 elected, it included General, Muslims, Europeans, Sikh.
  • 2. Budget Voting: Only 25% of the budget was subject to vote.
  • 3. Tenure: The members had a tenure of 5 years.
EXECUTIVE
  • 1. Chief Executive: The chief executive authority was the Governor-General.
  • 2. Indian Members: Out of the 6 members of the Viceroy's executive council, 3 were to be Indian members.
  • 3. Ordinance Power: The Governor-General could issue ordinances.
  • 4. Legislative Override: Governor-General could also enact a bill without the legislature's consent.
  • 5. Disallowance Power: Governor-General could disallow any question, adjournment motion or debate in the house.
VOTING RIGHT
Page 12
  • 1. Restricted Franchise: Franchise was restricted and there was no universal adult suffrage.
  • 2. Revenue Requirement: Voters should have paid land revenue of Rs.3000 or have property with rental value or have taxable income.
  • 3. Legislative Experience: Voters should possess previous experience in the legislative council.
  • 4. University Senate: Voters should be members of a university senate.
  • 5. Local Body Office: Voters should hold certain offices in the local bodies.
  • 6. Specific Titles: Voters should hold some specific titles.
INDIAN COUNCIL
  • 1. Council Size: It had at least 8 and a maximum of 12 members.
  • 2. Tenure: The members had a tenure of 5 years.
  • 3. Experience Requirement: Half of the members should have ten years of experience in public service in India.
  • 4. Indian Representation: There were to be 3 Indian members in the Council.
OTHER
  • 1. Public Service Commission: The act provided the establishment of a public service commission in India.
  • 2. Statutory Commission: The act also provided that after 10 years, a statutory commission would be set up to study the working of the government.
  • 3. High Commissioner: The act also created an office of the High Commissioner for India in London.
DRAWBACKS
  • 1. No Legislative Control: At the centre, the legislature had no control over the Governor-General and his executive council.
  • 2. Seat Allocation: Allocation of seats were based on importance of provinces (Punjab — military same as Bombay commercial).
  • 3. Limited Voting: The voting right was very limited which did not extend to the common man.
  • 4. Minister Limitations: The provincial ministers had no control over finances and over bureaucrats.
  • 5. Subject Division: Division of subjects was not satisfactory at the Centre.

Simon Commission
  • 1. Appointment: The Indian Statutory Commission {Simon Commission} was appointed in 1927 by Conservative government to evaluate the Montagu-Chelmsford reform.
  • 2. Leadership: It was headed by Sir John Simon and seven other members.
  • 3. Responsible Authority: Lord Birkenhead, the Secretary of State for India was responsible for setting up the Commission.
  • 4. Congress Boycott: The Congress Party decided to boycott the Commission at their session at Madras in 1927 because no Indian was on the committee.
  • 5. Report Publication: The Commission's report was published in 1930.
RECOMMENDATIONS
  • 1. Abolish Diarchy: To abolish the Diarchy in provinces.
  • 2. Communal Electorates: Withhold the separate communal electorates.
  • 3. Responsible Government: Extension of responsible government in provinces.
  • 4. Indian Federation: Creation of Indian federation of British India and Princely states.
IMPACT
  • 1. Non-Cooperation: The Indian National Congress decided not to cooperate with the Commission at Madras session in 1927.
  • 2. All Parties Conference: An All Parties Conference met in February 1928 to appoint a Subcommittee under the Chairmanship of Motilal Nehru to draft a memorandum.
  • 3. Simon Go Back: Simon Go Back was started.
  • 4. Young Turk Leaders: Jawaharlal Nehru, Vallabhbhai Patel and Subhas Chandra Bose emerged as the Young Turk leader.
  • 5. Round Table Conferences: The British government convened three round table conferences.
  • 6. White Paper: A White Paper on Constitutional Reforms was submitted for the consideration of Joint Select Committee of the British Parliament.
  • 7. Next Act: The recommendations of this committee were incorporated in the next Government of India Act of 1935.
NEHRU REPORT, 1928
  • 1. Origin: An All Parties Conference met in February 1928 to appoint a Subcommittee under the Chairmanship of Motilal Nehru to draft a memorandum.
  • 2. Subcommittee Setup: The subcommittee was set up to draft the Constitution on 19 May 1928.

Amid these challenges Nehru Report tried to cement the relations between Congress and Muslim League and present a unified front against Britishers through these following measures —

  • a. Reservation for Minorities: The Nehru report recommended reservation of seats for Muslims only in provinces where they were in a minority.

CRITICISM
  • 1. Not Close to Aspirations: The provision of the act was nowhere close to the Indian aspiration because Congress was demanding complete Independence but there was no trace of independence in this act.
  • 2. Fake Autonomy: The concept of Provincial autonomy was fake because real power is still in British government.

  • 4. Dyarchy Opposition: The Congress strongly opposed the introduction of the Dyarchy at centre because it failed at the Provincial level.

  • 6. Separate Electorate: Congress was against the system of separate electorate.

1. Nehru's Statement Context: The act for Indians seems like — Driving a car with all breaks but with no engine.

2. Criticism Context: While criticizing the Government of India Act 1935 Jawaharlal Nehru said the above statement. It was the lengthiest Act that was made before the year 1935. It proposed many salient features such as the creation of All India Federation, Provincial Autonomy, provision for safeguards and reservation, etc. These features were important for the Britishers but Indian politicians were not happy with these features.

  • a. No Independence: The provision of the Act of 1935 were nowhere close to the Indian aspiration because Congress was demanding complete Independence but there was no trace of independence in this act.
  • b. Farce Autonomy: Concept of provincial autonomy was farce because real power were still in the hand of British government.
  • c. Veto Power: Governor and Governor-General enjoy veto power.
Page 18
  • d. Dyarchy Failure: The Congress strongly opposed the introduction of dyarchy at the centre because it failed at provincial level.
  • e. Native State Freedom: Congress wanted all Native States must compulsorily join the Indian Federation but the act gave freedom to native States to decide whether they wanted to be part of the Federation or not.
  • f. Nomination Issue: Congress wanted representatives of native States in proposed federation should be elected by the public but the act proposed their nomination by the native States.
  • g. Separate Electorate Harm: Providing separate electorate to Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, etc, later on, turned out to be the tools for disintegrating India. Hence, this Act, in reality, did not help in curbing out the difficulties that had arisen in the Government of India Act, 1919.
  • h. Rigid Act: It was a rigid one. It could not be amended or modified by any Indian legislature, either it was Federal or Provincial Legislature. In the eyes of the people, this represented a disregard for the wishes of the people and the new Act did not make any amendments to this situation which was a pressing issue for the citizens.
  • i. British Dominion: It only showed the dominion of the British Government over the Indians only for their benefit.

3. Overall Assessment: Therefore, the Act was unable to provide the people with what it had proposed and had instead provided the people with changes that they considered unnecessary while the real concerns of the people were ignored by the British who aimed at expressing their dominance over the Indians and India through this Act.

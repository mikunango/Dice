INDIAN POLITY

CH8 - Directive Principles of State Policy

Part IV — Article 36 to 51

the kernel of the Indian Constitution

  • 1. Social & Economic Democracy: The concept behind the DPSP is not only establishing political democracy but also social and economic democracy in the state.
  • 2. Novel Features: According to Dr B R Ambedkar, these principles are 'novel features' of the Constitution.
  • 3. Source of DPSP: The source of the concept of DPSP is the Spanish Constitution from which it came in the Irish Constitution.
  • 4. GOI Act Influence: The Government of India Act also had some instructions related to this concept which became an important source of DPSP at that time.
  • 5. Sapru Report: Sapru Report of 1945 divided fundamental rights into justifiable and non-justifiable rights.
  • 6. Non-Justifiable Rights: Non-justifiable rights were included in part IV of the Constitution of India as Directive Principles of State Policy.
  • 7. Preamble Aim: The expression "Justice – Social, economic and political" that is mentioned in the preamble, is the ultimate aim of the DPSP.
FEATURES
SOCIALIST PRINCIPLES

1. Welfare State: Its ultimate aim is to provide social and economic justice to all its citizens so that the state can fulfil the criteria required for a welfare state.

2. Related Articles: Article 38, Article 39, Article 39 A, Article 41, Article 42, Article 43, Article 43 A and Article 47.

ARTICLE 38

1. Social Order: (1)The State shall strive to promote the welfare of the people by securing and protecting as effectively as it may a social order in which justice, social, economic and political, shall inform all the institutions of the national life.

2. Minimise Inequalities: (2) The State shall, in particular, strive to minimise the inequalities in income, and endeavour to eliminate inequalities in status, facilities and opportunities, not only amongst individuals but also amongst groups of people residing in different areas or engaged in different vocations.

  • 3. Subject Matter: Talks about Social, Political and Economic Justice.

ARTICLE 39

1. Policy Direction: The State shall, in particular, direct its policy towards securing—

  • 2. Adequate Livelihood: (a) that the citizens, men and women equally, have the right to an adequate means of livelihood;
  • 3. Common Good: (b) that the ownership and control of the material resources of the community are so distributed as best to sub serve the common good;
  • 4. Wealth Concentration: (c) that the operation of the economic system does not result in the concentration of wealth and means of production to the common detriment;
  • 5. Equal Pay: (d) that there is equal pay for equal work for both men and women;
  • 6. Worker Protection: (e) that the health and strength of workers, men and women, and the tender age of children are not abused and that citizens are not forced by economic necessity to enter avocations unsuited to their age or strength;
  • 7. Child Welfare: (f) that children are given opportunities and facilities to develop in a healthy manner and in conditions of freedom and dignity and that childhood and youth are protected against exploitation and against moral and material abandonment.
  • 8. State Policy Principles: Certain principles of policy to be followed by the State.
ARTICLE 39A

1. Equal Justice: The State shall secure that the operation of the legal system promotes justice, on a basis of equal opportunity, and shall, in particular, provide free legal aid, by suitable legislation or schemes or in any other way, to ensure that opportunities for securing justice are not denied to any citizen by reason of economic or other disabilities.

  • 2. Free Legal Aid: Equal justice and free legal aid.
ARTICLE 41

1. Right to Work: The State shall, within the limits of its economic capacity and development, make effective provision for securing the right to work, to education and to public assistance in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness and disablement, and in other cases of undeserved want.

  • 2. Welfare Government: Talks about Welfare Government.
ARTICLE 42

1. Humane Conditions: The State shall make provision for securing just and humane conditions of work and for maternity relief.

ARTICLE 43

1. Living Wage: The State shall endeavour to secure, by suitable legislation or economic organisation or in any other way, to all workers, agricultural, industrial or otherwise, work, a living wage, conditions of work ensuring a decent standard of life and full enjoyment of leisure and social and cultural opportunities

2. Cottage Industries: and, in particular, the State shall endeavour to promote cottage industries on an individual or co-operative basis in rural areas.

ARTICLE 43 A

1. Worker Participation: The State shall take steps, by suitable legislation or in any other way, to secure the participation of workers in the management of undertakings, establishments or other organisations engaged in any industry.

ARTICLE 47

1. Public Health: The State shall regard the raising of the level of nutrition and the standard of living of its people and the improvement of public health as among its primary duties and, in particular, the State shall endeavour to bring about prohibition of the consumption except for medicinal purposes of intoxicating drinks and of drugs which are injurious to health.

GANDHIAN PRINCIPLES

1. Gandhian Thought: Gandhian thinking promotes a non-violent social order. Swaraj ( Self-rule), Sarvodaya ( welfare for all ) and swavlamban (self-reliance) are the basic principles of Gandhian thought. His philosophy and actions guided not only our freedom movement but also the framing of the Indian Constitution. The following Directive Principles, in particular, reflect Gandhian thought:

  • 2. Weaker Sections: The state shall promote the educational and economic interests of the weaker sections of the society and in particular the interests of the scheduled castes and scheduled tribes. (Article 46)
  • 3. Village Panchayats: The state shall take steps to organise the village panchayats. These panchayats should be given such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as units of self-government. (Article 40)
  • 4. Alcoholic Drinks: The state shall make efforts to prevent the consumption of alcoholic drinks and other harmful drugs. (Article 47)
  • 5. Cottage Industries: The state shall try to promote cottage industries in rural areas. (Article 43)
  • 6. Co-operative Societies: The State shall endeavour to promote voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic control and professional management of co-operative societies. (Article 43 B)
  • 7. Animal Husbandry: The State shall endeavour to organise agriculture and animal husbandry on modern and scientific lines and shall, in particular, take steps for preserving and improving the breeds, and prohibiting the slaughter, of cows and calves and other milch and draught cattle. (Article 48)

ARTICLE 44

1. Uniform Civil Code: The State shall endeavour to secure for the citizens a uniform civil code throughout the territory of India.

ARTICLE 45

1. Early Childhood Care: The State shall endeavour to provide early childhood care and education for all children until they complete the age of SIX years.

  • 2. Compulsory Education: Free and compulsory education for the children until they are 14 years old.
ARTICLE 48 A

1. Environment Protection: The State shall endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wild life of the country.

ARTICLE 49

1. Monument Protection: It shall be the obligation of the State to protect every monument or place or object of artistic or historic interest, 2[declared by or under law made by Parliament] to be of national importance, from spoliation, disfigurement, destruction, removal, disposal or export, as the case may be.

ARTICLE 50

1. Judicial Separation: The State shall take steps to separate the judiciary from the executive in the public services of the State.

ARTICLE 51

1. International Peace: The State shall endeavour to—

  • 2. Peace & Security: (a) promote international peace and security;
  • 3. Just Relations: (b) maintain just and honourable relations between nations;
  • 4. International Law: (c) foster respect for international law and treaty obligations in the dealings of organised peoples with one another; and
  • 5. Dispute Arbitration: (d) encourage settlement of international disputes by arbitration.

SIGNIFICANCE
  • 1. Vox Populi: DPSP are based on Vox Populi (voice of the people).
  • 2. Welfare State: DPSP makes a responsibility of the State to develop the welfare state.
Page 88
  • 3. Policy Framework: DPSP helps the government to make policies and laws for the purpose of securing justice and welfare in the State.
  • 4. Supplementary to FR: DPSP are supplementary to Fundamental Rights which offers political rights and other freedoms.
  • 5. Guiding Principles: DPSP ensures that every Government will follow the set of principles in the form of DPSP while formulating its laws.
  • 6. Positive Directions: DPSP are positive directions for the State to secure social and economic justice.
  • 7. Court Interpretation: DPSP provide useful information to the courts in interpreting the provisions in the Constitution
CRITICAL ANALYSIS

1. Abstract Directives: Non-justiciable and abstract Directive Principles which may be safely ignored by the legislator...do not enhance the true prestige of a written constitution." M. Anantnarayan

2. Holy Wishes: Many critics have called these Directive Principles of State Policy as not better than 'New Year Greetings'. Even the rationale of inserting such high sounding promises has been questioned. It has been asserted that Directives are in the form of holy wishes having no legal sanction behind them.

  • 3. Unenforceable Directives: These principles are unenforceable directives, these principles do not carry any weight. Their violation or non-realization cannot be challenged in any court.
  • 4. No Time-Limit: The Constitution does not make them justiciable and neither fixes the time-limit within which these are to be secured. T. Shah describe it as a cheque payable by the bank at its convenience.
  • 5. Poorly Classified: These Directives are neither properly classified nor logically arranged. The declaration mixes up relatively unimportant issues with the most vital economic and social questions.
  • 6. Vague Formulation: The formulation of the Directive Principles of State Policy can hardly be considered inspiring. It is both vague and repetitive.
  • 7. Pious Promises: Directive principles are designed to serve as pious promises for creating an impression about the just and useful exercise of the power of the state. They aim to secure support by making promises and not through action.
  • 8. Foreign Foundations: The critics criticize Part IV also for the fact that most of the Directive Principles incorporated in it are based on age-old and foreign philosophical foundations which essentially the foundations of Fabian Socialism.
  • 9. Utopian Ideals: Some directives can only be successfully executed in a utopian society. They are therefore not ideal.
  • 10. Moral Promises: Gaining support through these vague moral promises and not through appropriate firm actions makes it useless.

11. Importance Upheld: However, despite the various criticisms, the importance and significance of the Directive Principle of State Policy cannot be undermined.
